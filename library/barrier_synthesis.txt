══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  THEORETICAL ANALYSIS: CHARACTERIZING THE L[1/3] BARRIER IN INTEGER
  FACTORING AND THE MATHEMATICAL REQUIREMENTS FOR BREAKING IT
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  A synthesis of ~360 explored algorithmic approaches to integer
  factoring, the five structural barriers they encounter, and a
  precise specification of what a hypothetical barrier-breaking
  mathematical object would need to satisfy.

  Date: 2025-03-25

══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


NOTATION AND CONVENTIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Throughout this document:

  N = pq         composite to be factored, p < q, both primes
  n = log N      bit-length of N (we use log = log₂ unless stated)

  L_N[α, c] = exp( c · (log N)^α · (log log N)^(1-α) )

  When c is suppressed:  L[α] = L_N[α, c] for some constant c > 0.

  The "L-exponent" of an algorithm is the infimum α such that the
  algorithm runs in time L[α, c] for some c.

  Current state of the art:
    - NFS (Number Field Sieve):        L[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3}]  ≈  L[1/3, 1.923]
    - Best heuristic variant:          L[1/3, (32/9)^{1/3}]  ≈  L[1/3, 1.526]
    - No classical algorithm achieves:  L[α] for any α < 1/3

  The five structural barriers (from the synthesis of ~360 approaches):

    B1. L[1/3] Wall — all known relation-finding strategies produce
        relations at cost ≥ L[1/3] per useful bit of information.
    B2. Archimedean/Non-Archimedean Gap — smoothness is an archimedean
        property (smallness of prime factors of norms), but the
        factoring secret (p) is a non-archimedean object (a prime
        divisor of N). No known technique bridges this gap at cost < L[1/3].
    B3. GF(2) Bottleneck — the exponent vectors of smooth relations
        live in GF(2)^π(B), and linear algebra over GF(2) cannot
        exploit multiplicative or analytic structure.
    B4. CRT Opacity — the Chinese Remainder Theorem isomorphism
        Z/NZ ≅ Z/pZ × Z/qZ is the fundamental structure hiding p,
        and any algorithm that requires "seeing through" this
        isomorphism to combine information faces an intrinsic barrier.
    B5. Z-Rigidity — the integers Z have very rigid algebraic
        structure; there are few "soft" algebraic operations available
        compared to, say, function fields over finite fields.


══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 1: PRECISE MATHEMATICAL SPECIFICATION OF A BARRIER-BREAKING
           STRUCTURE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We seek to characterize, in precise mathematical terms, what type of
object could break the L[1/3] barrier. We do NOT claim such an object
exists. We specify the REQUIREMENTS it must satisfy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.1  THE FIVE REQUIREMENTS (FORMAL STATEMENT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Let F be a hypothetical algorithm that factors N in time L[α] with
α < 1/3. Such an algorithm must, implicitly or explicitly, work with
a mathematical structure S(N) satisfying:

REQUIREMENT R1: COMPUTABILITY AND N-DEPENDENCE
──────────────────────────────────────────────
  S(N) must be a computable mathematical object associated to N
  (or more precisely, to Z/NZ) such that:

  (a) S(N) can be constructed from N in time ≤ L[α', c'] for some
      α' ≤ α.
  (b) S(N) carries structure that is sensitive to the factorization
      N = pq, meaning:
        S(N) ≇ S(N')  (in some appropriate categorical sense)
      for "most" N' with different factorizations.
  (c) Elements of S(N) can be represented in polynomial space
      poly(log N).

  Formally: S is a functor (or at least a map) from the category of
  composite integers (with some morphisms) to some computable category
  C, such that S preserves enough structure to distinguish
  factorizations.


REQUIREMENT R2: INFORMATION DENSITY
────────────────────────────────────
  There must exist a family of efficiently computable "queries"
  or "evaluations"

    φ_i : S(N) → Ω_i

  where Ω_i is some measurable output space, such that each
  evaluation reveals Ω(1) bits of information about the factorization
  (p, q) of N. More precisely:

  Let X = (p mod M) for some modulus M that is being reconstructed
  (or equivalently, let X encode some partial information about p).
  Then:

    H(X | φ_1(S(N)), ..., φ_k(S(N))) ≤ H(X) - Ω(k)

  That is, k evaluations reduce the entropy of our uncertainty about
  X by at least Ω(k) bits.

  CRITICAL CONSTRAINT: This must hold with > O(1) bits per evaluation,
  not merely O(1/poly(n)) bits. The reason: if each evaluation costs
  T and yields b bits, and we need n = log N bits total to determine
  p, then the algorithm costs at least (n/b) · T. To beat L[1/3],
  we need b · L[1/3] > n, i.e., b > n / L[1/3] — which for
  sub-L[1/3] evaluation cost means b must be Ω(1) or better.

  Remark: In NFS, each smooth relation yields exactly 1 bit of
  information about a dependency in the exponent vector space. The
  cost per relation is L[1/3], giving the L[1/3] bound. To beat this,
  either the cost per relation must decrease OR the bits per
  evaluation must increase.


REQUIREMENT R3: COMPOSABILITY
─────────────────────────────
  The outputs of multiple evaluations must COMPOSE — that is, there
  must be an efficiently computable combination operation

    ⊕ : Ω_i × Ω_j → Ω_{ij}

  (or more generally, a combination over any subset of evaluations)
  such that the combined output carries MORE information about p
  than any individual evaluation. Moreover:

  (a) The combination must be ALGEBRAIC, not merely set-theoretic.
      That is, ⊕ must have algebraic structure (group, ring, module,
      etc.) that enables efficient combination of many evaluations.

  (b) The combination must be SUB-ADDITIVE in cost: combining k
      evaluations should cost o(k · T_single), or at least the
      combined information should be super-additive: the k combined
      evaluations should yield ω(k · b_single) bits.

  (c) The composition must eventually SATURATE: after sufficiently
      many evaluations (polynomially many in log N), the combined
      information must determine p completely.

  Why this is hard: In NFS, composition happens via linear algebra
  over GF(2) — and the GF(2) structure is exactly what prevents
  super-additive information gain. Each new relation adds at most
  1 dimension to the null space.


REQUIREMENT R4: CRT-OPACITY AVOIDANCE
──────────────────────────────────────
  The most subtle requirement. The CRT isomorphism

    Z/NZ  ≅  Z/pZ × Z/qZ

  means that ANY element a ∈ Z/NZ is "secretly" a pair (a mod p,
  a mod q). The factoring problem is precisely that we cannot
  efficiently decompose this pair.

  The structure S(N) must be constructible WITHOUT knowing the CRT
  decomposition, yet the evaluations φ_i must extract information
  that is CORRELATED with the CRT decomposition.

  Formally: Define the "CRT barrier" as follows. Let

    π_p : Z/NZ → Z/pZ    (reduction mod p)

  be the unknown projection. Any algorithm that factors N must
  eventually compute or reconstruct π_p (or equivalently, p).
  The CRT-opacity barrier says:

    For any polynomial-time computable function f : Z/NZ → {0,1}^*,
    the mutual information I(f(a); a mod p) is "small" for random
    a ∈ Z/NZ, UNLESS f implicitly encodes p.

  More precisely, for most "natural" functions f:

    I(f(a); a mod p | N) ≤ O(1/N^{1/2})

  The barrier-breaking structure must circumvent this, likely by:

  (a) Working with a STRUCTURED (non-random) subset of Z/NZ where
      the CRT decomposition partially "leaks" through algebraic
      relations, OR
  (b) Operating in an EXTENSION or LIFT of Z/NZ where the CRT
      decomposition has a different character, OR
  (c) Using a GLOBAL property of Z/NZ (such as its multiplicative
      group structure, or a derived algebraic-geometric object)
      rather than pointwise evaluations.

  CRITICAL OBSERVATION: Approaches (a) and (b) are what NFS does
  (via number field embeddings). The L[1/3] barrier arises because
  the "leakage" per structured element is O(1) bits at cost L[1/3]
  per element. Approach (c) — using global structure — is the
  most promising but least understood direction.


REQUIREMENT R5: SUB-L[1/3] CONSTRUCTIBILITY
───────────────────────────────────────────
  Every component of the algorithm must be constructible in time
  < L[1/3]:

  (a) The structure S(N) itself must be constructible in time L[α']
      with α' < 1/3.
  (b) Each evaluation φ_i must be computable in time L[α''] with
      α'' < 1/3.
  (c) The composition operation ⊕ must be computable in polynomial
      time in log N.
  (d) The final extraction of p from the composed information must
      be computable in polynomial time in log N.

  This is the "findability" requirement. It rules out many beautiful
  mathematical structures that carry the right information but
  cannot be efficiently computed.

  EXAMPLE OF FAILURE: The class group Cl(Q(√-N)) carries information
  about the factorization of N (its order h(-N) is related to p and q
  via genus theory). But computing h(-N) exactly is itself as hard as
  factoring N by the best known methods.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.2  THE INFORMATION-THEORETIC CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A fundamental quantitative constraint ties these requirements together.
To factor N, an algorithm must extract n/2 = (log N)/2 bits of
information about p. If the algorithm:

  - Makes k queries/evaluations
  - Each costs time T
  - Each yields b bits of information about p

Then:

  Total time ≥ k · T
  Total info ≥ k · b ≥ n/2

  ⟹  k ≥ n/(2b)
  ⟹  Total time ≥ (n · T) / (2b)

For the algorithm to run in time L[α] with α < 1/3, we need:

  (n · T) / (2b) ≤ L[α]

Case analysis:

  CASE A: b = O(1) bits per evaluation (like NFS).
    Then T ≤ L[α] · 2b/n ≈ L[α]/n.
    Since n = poly(log N) grows slower than any L[ε], this means
    T ≤ L[α] — each evaluation must cost at most L[α].
    For NFS: T = L[1/3], b = O(1), giving L[1/3].
    To beat L[1/3] with b = O(1), need T < L[1/3].

  CASE B: b = ω(1) bits per evaluation (SUPER-LINEAR INFORMATION).
    Then T can be as large as L[α] · b, which allows more expensive
    individual evaluations if they yield proportionally more information.
    This is the "information-dense" regime that no known structure
    achieves.

  CASE C: b = poly(log N) bits per evaluation (POLYNOMIAL INFO).
    Then k = O(1) evaluations suffice, and T ≤ L[α] total.
    This would be a "few expensive queries" algorithm — essentially
    computing the factorization in one or few steps.
    This is the regime of hypothetical polynomial-time algorithms
    (which would give L[0]).

The barrier-breaking structure most plausibly operates in CASE B:
yielding super-constant but sub-polynomial information per evaluation,
at a cost that makes the total sub-L[1/3].

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1.3  THE ALGEBRAIC CONSTRAINT: WHAT TYPE OF OBJECT?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Given requirements R1-R5, what algebraic species could S(N) belong to?

CONSTRAINT FROM R1 + R5 (computability):
  S(N) must live in a category where objects are polynomial-size
  and morphisms are polynomial-time computable.
  This rules out: infinite-dimensional objects, non-constructive
  objects, objects requiring exponential-size representations.

CONSTRAINT FROM R2 + R3 (information + composability):
  The evaluations must have algebraic structure enabling composition.
  The most natural frameworks:
    (i)   Group-theoretic: evaluations live in a group, composition = group law
    (ii)  Ring-theoretic: evaluations live in a ring, composition = ring operations
    (iii) Module-theoretic: evaluations live in a module over a known ring
    (iv)  Category-theoretic: evaluations are morphisms, composition = composition
    (v)   Cohomological: evaluations are cocycles, composition = cup product

CONSTRAINT FROM R4 (CRT-opacity avoidance):
  The structure must "see through" the CRT decomposition without
  knowing it. This is the most restrictive constraint.

  Known structures that partially see through CRT:
    - Quadratic residuosity: 1 bit (Jacobi vs actual residuosity)
    - Smooth relations: O(1) bits per relation
    - Elliptic curve group order: carries info but costs L[1/3] to extract
    - Class group structure: carries info but costs ≥ L[1/3] to compute

COMBINED CONSTRAINT:
  S(N) must be an algebraic object that:
    (1) Is polynomial-size and polynomial-time constructible from N
    (2) Has a natural "evaluation" operation yielding > O(1) bits
        about the CRT decomposition
    (3) Has algebraic composition of evaluations
    (4) Does not require knowing p for construction or composition
    (5) Is constructible in time < L[1/3]

  This is an extraordinarily restrictive set of requirements, which
  explains why 360 approaches have all failed.


══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 2: WHY EACH KNOWN MATHEMATICAL STRUCTURE FAILS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We systematically examine every major class of mathematical structure
that has been proposed or explored for factoring, and identify exactly
which requirement(s) each one fails.

The ~360 explored approaches cluster into approximately 20 structural
families. We analyze each family below.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.1  NUMBER FIELDS AND NORMS (NFS family)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Number fields K = Q(α) with a homomorphism φ: Z[α] → Z/NZ.
ALGORITHMS: NFS, SNFS, GNFS, tower NFS, multiple NFS.

HOW IT WORKS:
  Choose f(x) ∈ Z[x] with f(m) ≡ 0 (mod N). Then the map α ↦ m
  gives a ring homomorphism Z[α] → Z/NZ. Elements a - bα ∈ Z[α]
  that are smooth (as ideals) AND whose norm N_{K/Q}(a - bα) is
  smooth (as integers) give relations.

WHICH REQUIREMENTS IT SATISFIES:
  ✓ R1 (computability): Number fields are constructible in poly time.
  ✓ R3 (composability): Relations compose via addition in GF(2)^k.
  ✓ R4 (CRT-opacity): Construction doesn't need p.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R2 (information density): Each smooth relation yields O(1) bits.
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): The fundamental issue is NORM SIZE.

DETAILED FAILURE ANALYSIS:
  For a - bα with |a|, |b| ≤ M, the norm N_{K/Q}(a - bα) has size
  roughly M^d where d = deg(f). The probability that this norm is
  B-smooth is approximately:

    ψ(M^d, B) / M^d ≈ u^{-u}  where u = d·log(M)/log(B)

  Optimization: choosing d ≈ (log N / log log N)^{1/3} and
  B ≈ L[1/3, β], M ≈ L[1/3, μ] gives the L[1/3] complexity.

  THE BARRIER: The norm N_{K/Q}(·) is a POLYNOMIAL of degree d in
  the coefficients. Its size grows as M^d. To get smoothness
  probability u^{-u} with u = O(1), we need B ≈ M^d, but then
  we need L[1/3]-many relations to span the space.

  No choice of number field avoids this: the norm is always a
  degree-d polynomial, and d must grow with N to keep norms
  "reasonably sized" relative to N. This is a theorem, not a
  limitation of current methods.

  WHAT WOULD FIX IT: A "norm" map whose output is SUBEXPONENTIAL
  in the input coefficients, rather than polynomial-exponential.
  No such map exists in number fields (it is a consequence of
  the product formula / Artin-Whaples theorem).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.2  MULTIPLICATIVE GROUP ORDERS AND ECM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: The multiplicative group (Z/NZ)*, elliptic curves E(Z/NZ),
higher-dimensional abelian varieties A(Z/NZ).

ALGORITHMS: Pollard p-1, Williams p+1, ECM, HECM.

HOW IT WORKS:
  Compute a^M mod N for M = lcm(1, ..., B). If p-1 is B-smooth,
  then a^M ≡ 1 (mod p) but a^M ≢ 1 (mod q) (probably), so
  gcd(a^M - 1, N) = p.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R2 (information density): Each computation either succeeds
    completely (if p-1 is smooth) or yields ZERO bits.
  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): The group order |E(Z/pZ)| depends on p,
    and the algorithm only works if this order has a special form.
    For random p, the probability that p-1 (or |E(F_p)|) is
    B-smooth is ≈ u^{-u} with u = log(p)/log(B) ≈ log(N)/log(B).

  The fundamental issue: GROUP ORDER IS A PROPERTY OF p, NOT OF N.
  We cannot compute |E(Z/pZ)| without knowing p. We can only
  compute in E(Z/NZ), which has order lcm(|E(Z/pZ)|, |E(Z/qZ)|),
  and this lcm structure hides the individual orders via CRT.

  ECM achieves L_p[1/2] for finding a factor p, which is
  subexponential in p but exponential in log(N)/2 when p ≈ √N.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.3  LATTICE METHODS AND LATTICE REDUCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Integer lattices L ⊂ Z^n, their short vectors, and
reduction theory (LLL, BKZ, HKZ).

ALGORITHMS: Lattice-based relation finding, Coppersmith's method,
lattice sieving, approximate GCD.

HOW IT WORKS:
  Many factoring subproblems reduce to finding short vectors in
  lattices derived from N. Coppersmith's method finds small roots
  of modular polynomial equations, which can factor N when partial
  information about p is known.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): Lattice reduction achieves polynomial
    approximation factors in polynomial time (LLL), but the
    lattices arising from factoring have dimension d that must
    grow with N. Finding shortest vectors in dimension d takes
    time 2^{Θ(d)}, and the required dimension d for factoring-
    relevant lattices is ω(polylog(N)).

  ✗ R2 (information density): Lattice methods provide information
    proportional to the "quality" of the short vector found, but
    this quality is limited by the lattice dimension and the
    Gaussian heuristic. The information per lattice reduction
    operation is O(1) bits toward factoring.

  COPPERSMITH'S EXCEPTION: When ≥ n/2 bits of p are known,
  Coppersmith's method factors N in polynomial time. This is
  exactly the Case C regime from Section 1.2 — but it requires
  external information about p that must itself be found.

  FUNDAMENTAL BARRIER: Lattice methods are excellent at converting
  "approximate" information into "exact" information (the
  Coppersmith principle). But they cannot GENERATE the initial
  approximate information about p from N alone at sub-L[1/3] cost.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.4  ALGEBRAIC GEOMETRY: CURVES AND HIGHER-DIMENSIONAL VARIETIES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Algebraic curves C/Z/NZ, Jacobians J(C), abelian
varieties, schemes over Spec(Z/NZ).

ALGORITHMS: Hyperelliptic curve method, higher-genus analogues of
ECM, Jacobian-based methods.

HOW IT WORKS:
  Generalize ECM to curves of higher genus g. The Jacobian J(C)
  has dimension g, so its group order over F_p is ≈ p^g, with
  smoothness probability that depends on the distribution of
  eigenvalues of Frobenius.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): The Jacobian J(C)(Z/NZ) decomposes via CRT:
    J(C)(Z/NZ) ≅ J(C)(Z/pZ) × J(C)(Z/qZ)
    and this decomposition is just as opaque as for Z/NZ itself.
    The group order |J(C)(F_p)| depends on p through the
    characteristic polynomial of Frobenius, which cannot be
    computed without knowing p.

  ✗ R2 (in the ECM regime): For genus g curves, the group order
    is ≈ p^g, and smoothness probability improves, but the time
    to compute in the Jacobian also grows as poly(g). The
    optimized complexity for finding a factor p is:

      L_p[1/2, 1/√(2g)]    (decreasing with g)

    But as g → ∞, we need genus g ≈ √(log p) curves, and the
    overhead of computing in high-genus Jacobians negates the
    improvement. The complexity stays in the L_p[1/2] class.

  ✗ R5 (when seeking L_N[1/3]): To match NFS's L_N[1/3], the
    algebraic geometry approach would need to somehow extract
    information about BOTH p and q simultaneously from the
    geometry of C over Z/NZ. No known mechanism does this without
    implicitly knowing the CRT decomposition.

  WHAT WOULD FIX IT: An algebraic-geometric invariant of C/Z/NZ
  that is:
    (a) sensitive to the factorization of N
    (b) computable without the CRT decomposition
    (c) more informative than individual group orders
  Candidates like the "Hasse-Weil zeta function of C over Z/NZ"
  are not even well-defined objects — the Weil conjectures assume
  curves over FIELDS, not over Z/NZ.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.5  CLASS FIELD THEORY AND CLASS GROUPS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Class groups Cl(K) for number fields K related to N,
Hilbert class fields, ring class fields, and genus theory.

ALGORITHMS: Class group computation, relation-finding in Cl(K),
subexponential discrete logarithm in Cl(K).

HOW IT WORKS:
  For K = Q(√(-N)), the class group Cl(K) has an explicit
  connection to the factorization of N through GENUS THEORY:

    Cl(K) / Cl(K)^2  ≅  (Z/2Z)^{t-1}

  where t is the number of distinct prime factors of the discriminant
  of K. The 2-rank of Cl(K) reveals the number of prime factors of N.

  More refined information: the actual group structure of Cl(K)
  determines p and q through the theory of binary quadratic forms.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): Computing the full class group Cl(Q(√(-N)))
    requires finding smooth relations among ideals, which is
    essentially the same problem as NFS. The best known algorithm
    computes Cl(K) in time L[1/2] for general K, or L[1/3] when
    using NFS-like techniques in the function field analogy.

  ✗ R2 (information density in genus theory): Genus theory gives
    exactly t-1 bits about the factorization (where t = number of
    prime factors). For N = pq, this is just 1 bit — the Jacobi
    symbol, which is trivially computable. Getting MORE than 1 bit
    from genus theory requires computing in the class group
    BEYOND the 2-torsion, which costs L[1/3].

  The deep irony: the class group CONTAINS the full factorization
  of N (one can recover p from the structure of Cl(Q(√(-N)))), but
  COMPUTING this structure is as hard as factoring N directly. The
  information is there but inaccessible.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.6  p-ADIC AND LOCAL METHODS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: p-adic integers Z_p, p-adic analysis, Hensel lifting,
Newton polygons, Witt vectors.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  If we knew p, we could embed Z/NZ into Z_p and use p-adic
  analysis. The idea is to find p-adic invariants computable
  from N alone.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R1 (N-dependence without p): p-adic methods intrinsically
    require knowing which prime p to complete to. Z/NZ does not
    have a canonical embedding into any p-adic ring without
    knowing a factor. This is a CATEGORICAL obstruction:

      Spec(Z/NZ) = {(p), (q)}  (two closed points)

    and choosing a local completion means choosing a point,
    which IS the factorization.

  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): This is the same barrier from a different
    angle. The "local-global" structure of Z/NZ

      Z/NZ ↪ Z_p × Z_q    (via CRT)

    cannot be decomposed without knowing p and q. p-adic methods
    are inherently "local" and the factoring problem is precisely
    the problem of passing from global to local.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.7  ANALYTIC NUMBER THEORY AND L-FUNCTIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Riemann zeta function ζ(s), Dirichlet L-functions L(s,χ),
Dedekind zeta functions ζ_K(s), automorphic L-functions.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  The factorization of N is "encoded" in the analytic properties
  of L-functions. For example:

    ζ_{Q(√(-N))}(s) = ζ(s) · L(s, χ_{-N})

  and the special value L(1, χ_{-N}) determines the class number
  h(-N), which determines the factorization.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): Computing L(1, χ_{-N}) to sufficient
    precision requires evaluating ≈ √N terms of the L-series
    (via the approximate functional equation), which costs
    O(√N) = O(2^{n/2}) — exponential time.

  ✗ R2 (information density via partial sums): Partial sums of
    the L-series give approximations to L(1, χ_{-N}), but each
    term contributes O(1/T) bits of precision when T terms are
    summed. To get enough precision to determine p, we need
    T ≈ √N terms.

  More sophisticated approaches (e.g., using zeros of L-functions,
  moment methods) run into the same barrier: the analytic
  information about p is distributed across ≈ √N Fourier
  coefficients, and there is no known shortcut to aggregate them.

  CONNECTION TO RIEMANN HYPOTHESIS: Even assuming GRH, the best
  known improvement to L-series evaluation is polylogarithmic.
  GRH helps with CERTIFICATION of factors (Miller's test) but
  not with FINDING them.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.8  REPRESENTATION THEORY AND AUTOMORPHIC FORMS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Representations of GL_n(Z/NZ), automorphic forms on
reductive groups, Hecke operators, Langlands program connections.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  The representation theory of GL_n(Z/NZ) reflects the CRT
  decomposition:

    Rep(GL_n(Z/NZ)) ≅ Rep(GL_n(Z/pZ)) ⊗ Rep(GL_n(Z/qZ))

  Perhaps some representation-theoretic invariant could detect
  the factorization.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): The decomposition of representations mirrors
    the CRT decomposition EXACTLY. Any representation-theoretic
    quantity that distinguishes the factorization must "see through"
    CRT, which is the factoring problem itself.

  ✗ R5 (constructibility): Computing representation-theoretic
    invariants of GL_n(Z/NZ) for n ≥ 2 involves working with
    objects of size |GL_n(Z/NZ)| ≈ N^{n²}, which is exponential.
    Even for n = 1, we are back to the multiplicative group
    (Z/NZ)*, whose structure is determined by CRT.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.9  FUNCTION FIELDS AND THE FUNCTION FIELD ANALOGY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: F_q[T] (polynomial rings over finite fields), function
fields F_q(C) of curves, Drinfeld modules.

WHY THIS IS INTERESTING:
  Factoring in F_q[T] (splitting a polynomial into irreducible factors)
  is in POLYNOMIAL TIME (Berlekamp's algorithm, Cantor-Zassenhaus).
  The function field analogy suggests that structures available over
  F_q might have Z-analogues that help with integer factoring.

  More precisely: the analogue of NFS over function fields achieves
  L[1/3] as well (Adleman-Huang for function field DLP), suggesting
  the L[1/3] barrier might be a phenomenon of the analogy itself.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (Z-rigidity): The fundamental reason factoring is easy over
    F_q[T] is the FROBENIUS ENDOMORPHISM: for f(T) ∈ F_q[T],

      f(T)^q ≡ f(T^q) (mod p(T))   for irreducible p(T)

    This gives a polynomial-time computable "fingerprint" of p(T).
    There is NO analogue of Frobenius for Z. The integers lack an
    endomorphism of this type, which is the Z-rigidity barrier (B5).

  ✗ R1 (the analogy doesn't transfer): Drinfeld modules, Anderson
    T-motives, and other function field constructions have no direct
    Z-analogue that would help with factoring. The "translation
    dictionary" between function fields and number fields (the
    Langlands program, the Weil-Deligne group, etc.) is structural,
    not algorithmic — it preserves theorems but not algorithms.

  WHAT WE LEARN: The function field case tells us that the L[1/3]
  barrier is NOT an information-theoretic necessity. It is a
  consequence of the ARITHMETIC STRUCTURE of Z versus F_q[T].
  Specifically, Z lacks:
    (a) A Frobenius endomorphism
    (b) A finite base field to reduce to
    (c) A geometric structure (curves, surfaces) with cohomological tools

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.10  QUANTUM APPROACHES (SHOR AND BEYOND)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Quantum Fourier transform, hidden subgroup problem,
quantum walks, quantum polynomial systems.

ALGORITHMS: Shor's algorithm, Regev's recent improvement, quantum
NFS, quantum lattice sieving.

HOW SHOR WORKS:
  Shor's algorithm factors N in poly(n) quantum time by:
  (a) Computing the order r of a random a ∈ (Z/NZ)*
  (b) Using r to find p via gcd(a^{r/2} ± 1, N)

  The key insight: order-finding reduces to the HIDDEN SUBGROUP
  PROBLEM (HSP) in the abelian group Z, which quantum computers
  solve efficiently via the quantum Fourier transform (QFT).

WHICH CLASSICAL REQUIREMENT IT SATISFIES:
  ✓ R2 (information density): Each quantum order-finding yields
    O(n) bits of information about the group structure (the full
    order r, which determines p with probability ≥ 1/2).
  ✓ R3 (composability): Multiple runs compose trivially (one run
    suffices with high probability).
  ✓ R4 (CRT-opacity): QFT "sees through" CRT because it can
    detect periodicity in the SUPERPOSITION a^0, a^1, ..., a^{r-1},
    which encodes r = ord(a) in (Z/NZ)* without knowing p.

WHICH CLASSICAL REQUIREMENT IT "VIOLATES":
  ✗ R5 (classical sub-L[1/3]): Shor's algorithm requires a
    QUANTUM computer. The QFT is not classically simulable in
    sub-exponential time.

LESSON FOR CLASSICAL ALGORITHMS:
  Shor's algorithm demonstrates that R4 (CRT-opacity) is NOT an
  absolute barrier — it can be circumvented by accessing the
  correct mathematical structure (periodicity detection via
  superposition). The question is whether there exists a CLASSICAL
  mechanism that achieves something analogous.

  CRITICAL INSIGHT: Shor's algorithm extracts O(n) bits from ONE
  evaluation by accessing the ENTIRE periodic structure of a^k mod N
  simultaneously. Classically, we can only evaluate a^k for one k
  at a time, getting O(1) bits. The quantum advantage is precisely
  in INFORMATION DENSITY per evaluation (R2).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.11  SMOOTH NUMBERS AND SIEVING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: B-smooth numbers, the Dickman rho function ρ(u),
sieving theory, smooth number distribution.

ALGORITHMS: Quadratic sieve, CFRAC, Dixon's method, sieving in NFS.

THE UNIVERSAL BOTTLENECK:
  Almost all subexponential factoring algorithms reduce to:
    (1) Find B-smooth numbers in a structured set S ⊂ Z/NZ
    (2) Combine smooth relations to find a square mod N
    (3) Extract p from gcd(x² - y², N) = gcd(x-y, N) · gcd(x+y, N)

  The smoothness bottleneck: the probability that a random integer
  m ≤ X is B-smooth is ρ(u) where u = log(X)/log(B) and ρ is
  the Dickman function satisfying ρ(u) ∼ u^{-u} for large u.

  Optimization: the sieve region has X ≈ N^{1/d} (for degree-d
  polynomials) and we choose B = L[1/3, β]. This gives:

    u ≈ (1/d) · (log N / log B) ≈ (1/d) · (log N)^{2/3} / (log log N)^{1/3}

  and the smoothness probability ρ(u) ≈ L[-1/3, ...], meaning we
  need L[1/3] sieve operations to find one smooth relation.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R2 + R5 jointly: The smoothness probability ρ(u) ∼ u^{-u}
    is a UNIVERSAL bound — it applies to any polynomial evaluated
    at integers in a range. There is no polynomial p(x₁,...,x_k)
    with roots mod N whose values are smoother than ρ predicts,
    unless the polynomial has special structure (e.g., degree 1,
    which gives trivial smoothness at too-large cost).

  This is perhaps the deepest barrier: smoothness is governed by
  the ANALYTIC properties of the integers (prime distribution),
  and no algebraic trick changes the analytic smoothness probability.

  POTENTIAL LOOPHOLE: Non-polynomial structured sets. If the
  "sieve target" is not a polynomial evaluation but some other
  structured set, the smoothness probability might differ. But
  all known constructions of structured sets in Z/NZ ultimately
  reduce to polynomial evaluations after algebraic manipulation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.12  LINEAR ALGEBRA OVER GF(2) AND STRUCTURED MATRICES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Sparse matrices over GF(2), structured linear algebra
(Block Lanczos, Block Wiedemann), the GF(2) null space.

THE GF(2) BOTTLENECK (Barrier B3):
  After collecting smooth relations, factoring reduces to:

    Find v ∈ GF(2)^k such that Mv = 0

  where M is the exponent matrix of smooth relations. The matrix
  M has dimensions ≈ π(B) × (π(B) + ε), and the null space
  computation takes time O(π(B)^2) (Block Wiedemann) or O(π(B)·w)
  where w is the average row weight.

  With B = L[1/3, β], this linear algebra costs L[2/3, ...],
  which would DOMINATE the sieving cost — EXCEPT that the matrix
  is sparse (row weight O(polylog N)), and structured algorithms
  exploit this, bringing the cost down to L[1/3, ...] matching
  the sieve.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R3 (composability, in a deep sense): The GF(2) structure
    destroys multiplicative information. When we reduce exponent
    vectors mod 2, we lose the actual exponents — we only know
    PARITY. This means:

    (a) We cannot use multiplicative structure (e.g., the fact
        that smooth numbers have small prime factors with specific
        exponent distributions) to speed up the linear algebra.
    (b) We cannot compose relations "multiplicatively" — only
        additively (XOR) in the exponent space.
    (c) The problem is combinatorial (find a GF(2) dependency)
        rather than algebraic (exploit ring structure).

  WHAT WOULD FIX IT: A framework where the "relations" live in
  a richer algebraic structure than GF(2)^k — perhaps a module
  over a ring with more structure, where the combination operation
  is more efficient. This connects to the question of whether
  factoring can be reduced to a problem over a field of
  characteristic ≠ 2, or over a ring with non-trivial units.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.13  QUANTUM-INSPIRED CLASSICAL APPROACHES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Tensor networks, matrix product states, simulated
quantum circuits, dequantized algorithms.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  Attempt to classically simulate enough of Shor's algorithm to
  factor N. Since Shor works by exploiting the STRUCTURE of the
  QFT applied to periodic functions, perhaps this structure can
  be captured classically.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): The quantum state in Shor's algorithm
    after the modular exponentiation step is:

      |ψ⟩ = (1/√r) Σ_{j=0}^{r-1} |j·(N/r)⟩|a^j mod N⟩

    This state has ENTANGLEMENT ENTROPY proportional to log(r) ≈ n.
    Classically simulating this requires representing an exponentially
    large state, which costs 2^{Ω(n)} time/space.

  More precisely: the tensor network representing the modular
  exponentiation circuit has bond dimension 2^{Ω(n)}, and no
  known compression brings this below subexponential.

  Tang-style dequantization (for recommendation systems, etc.) does
  not apply because factoring is not a "low-rank" problem in the
  relevant sense.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.14  COMBINATORIAL AND GRAPH-THEORETIC APPROACHES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Cayley graphs of (Z/NZ)*, expander graphs, spectral
methods on graphs derived from N.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  Construct a graph G(N) whose spectral properties (eigenvalues of
  adjacency matrix, expansion, etc.) encode the factorization of N.
  Use spectral algorithms (random walks, spectral clustering) to
  extract this information.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): The Cayley graph of (Z/NZ)* decomposes
    spectrally via CRT:

      Spec(Cay((Z/NZ)*)) = Spec(Cay((Z/pZ)*)) ⊗ Spec(Cay((Z/qZ)*))

    where ⊗ denotes the tensor product of spectra. Computing the
    individual spectral components requires knowing p.

  ✗ R2 (information density): Random walks on Cayley graphs of
    (Z/NZ)* mix in time O(log N) = O(n), but the mixing gives
    UNIFORM distribution — it erases information about the graph
    structure rather than revealing it. Spectral algorithms extract
    eigenvalues, but the relevant eigenvalues (those of the
    "p-component") are hidden in the tensor product.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.15  ALGEBRAIC COMPLEXITY AND ARITHMETIC CIRCUITS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Arithmetic circuits, algebraic computation trees,
Straight-line programs for computing in Z/NZ.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  Lower bounds on arithmetic circuit complexity could show that
  certain computations over Z/NZ require Ω(f(N)) operations,
  which would establish barriers. Conversely, efficient circuits
  for "factoring-related" functions could break barriers.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ This is not a constructive approach but a BARRIERS approach.
    No arithmetic circuit lower bound beyond linear is known for
    EXPLICIT functions (the "P vs NC" type gap). We cannot even
    prove that multiplication requires super-linear circuit size,
    let alone that factoring requires L[1/3] circuit size.

  ✗ R5 (in the constructive direction): Known efficient arithmetic
    circuits over Z/NZ compute POLYNOMIAL functions, and evaluating
    polynomial functions mod N does not escape the smoothness
    bottleneck (Section 2.11).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.16  ARITHMETIC GEOMETRY: MOTIVES AND COHOMOLOGY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Étale cohomology H^i(Spec(Z/NZ), F), motivic cohomology,
crystalline cohomology, the motivic Galois group.

WHY THIS IS INTERESTING:
  The deepest invariants of Z/NZ come from arithmetic geometry.
  The étale fundamental group π₁(Spec(Z/NZ)) "knows" the
  factorization: Spec(Z/NZ) = Spec(Z/pZ) ⊔ Spec(Z/qZ) (as
  schemes), so π₁ = π₁(Spec(F_p)) × π₁(Spec(F_q)) = Ẑ × Ẑ,
  and knowing this decomposition is knowing p and q.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): Computing étale cohomology groups of
    Spec(Z/NZ) (or of varieties over Z/NZ) requires either:
    (a) Knowing the decomposition Spec(Z/NZ) = Spec(F_p) ⊔ Spec(F_q),
        which IS the factorization, or
    (b) Working with cohomological invariants that are "blind" to the
        decomposition, which reduces to the same barriers as
        Section 2.8 (representation theory).

  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): Cohomology is FUNCTORIAL, meaning:
    H^i(Spec(Z/NZ), F) ≅ H^i(Spec(F_p), F) ⊕ H^i(Spec(F_q), F)
    and this decomposition mirrors CRT exactly. ANY cohomological
    invariant of Spec(Z/NZ) decomposes as a product of invariants
    of Spec(F_p) and Spec(F_q), and computing the components requires
    the factorization.

  THE MOTIVIC DREAM: Is there a "motivic" invariant of Z/NZ that
  does NOT decompose via CRT? This would require a non-functorial
  construction, which goes against the foundations of algebraic
  geometry. It seems unlikely but cannot be absolutely ruled out.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.17  ADDITIVE COMBINATORICS AND SUM-PRODUCT PHENOMENA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Sumsets A+A, product sets A·A, sum-product estimates
in Z/NZ, Freiman-type theorems.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  The sum-product phenomenon says that for A ⊂ Z/pZ,
  max(|A+A|, |A·A|) ≫ |A|^{1+ε}. The point: addition and
  multiplication "disagree" in Z/pZ, and this disagreement
  might be exploitable for factoring since the CRT decomposition
  treats addition and multiplication differently.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): Sum-product estimates over Z/NZ involve
    BOTH the additive and multiplicative structure, but they hold
    for ANY ring, not specifically for Z/NZ with its particular
    factorization. The sum-product phenomenon is a GENERIC property
    of rings, not one that distinguishes Z/NZ from Z/N'Z.

  ✗ R2 (information density): Known sum-product results give
    EXISTENCE results (certain sets must be large) but not
    CONSTRUCTIVE information about p. Converting sum-product
    bounds into a factoring algorithm would require constructing
    specific sets A where the sum-product behavior differs between
    the "mod p" and "mod q" components — which again requires CRT
    decomposition to verify.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.18  DERANDOMIZATION AND PSEUDORANDOMNESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Pseudorandom generators, extractors, expander graphs,
the hardness-randomness connection.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  If factoring is hard, then the Blum-Blum-Shub generator (x → x²
  mod N) is a secure PRG. Conversely, if we could BREAK this PRG
  (distinguish its output from random), we could factor N. Perhaps
  structural properties of the PRG's output can be detected
  without factoring N.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ This is CIRCULAR: breaking BBS is as hard as factoring N
    (under standard assumptions). Any efficient BBS distinguisher
    IS a factoring algorithm, so this doesn't provide a new angle.

  ✗ R1 (the structure IS the problem): The "structure" here is
    the quadratic residuosity structure of Z/NZ, which is exactly
    the factoring problem in disguise.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.19  HOMOMORPHIC ENCRYPTION AND BOOTSTRAPPING (COMPUTATIONAL)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) circuits,
bootstrapping, noise management, the LWE/RLWE connection.

HOW IT WORKS (hypothetically):
  FHE allows computation on encrypted data. If we could "encrypt"
  the factoring problem and solve it homomorphically using structure
  that is visible in the encrypted domain...

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (this doesn't help): FHE computes the same functions as
    non-FHE, just on encrypted inputs. It doesn't provide access
    to new mathematical structure — it provides PRIVACY of inputs.
    Running NFS under FHE still costs L[1/3].

  ✗ Fundamentally: FHE is a tool for COMPUTATION, not for
    MATHEMATICS. It doesn't reveal new structural properties of
    Z/NZ; it merely allows existing computations to be done in
    different settings.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.20  TOPOLOGICAL AND HOMOTOPICAL APPROACHES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRUCTURES: Homotopy type theory, higher categories, topological
invariants of Spec(Z/NZ), perfectoid spaces.

WHY THIS IS INTERESTING:
  Perfectoid spaces (Scholze) have revolutionized arithmetic geometry
  by providing new tools for "tilting" between characteristic 0 and
  characteristic p. The perfectoid approach might provide a bridge
  between the function field world (where factoring is easy) and
  the number field world.

WHICH REQUIREMENT IT FAILS:
  ✗ R5 (sub-L[1/3] cost): Perfectoid methods work over COMPLETE
    fields (e.g., Q_p^, the completed algebraic closure of Q_p).
    Constructing the relevant perfectoid spaces requires choosing
    a prime p — which IS the factoring problem. The tilting
    equivalence

      Perf_{Q_p} ≅ Perf_{F_p((t))}

    relates the p-adic world to the function field world, but
    only AFTER p is known.

  ✗ R4 (CRT-opacity): There is no known "perfectoid space over
    Z/NZ" that doesn't decompose via CRT. The entire perfectoid
    machinery is local (centered at a prime p), and globalizing
    it over Spec(Z/NZ) without knowing the primes is an unsolved
    (and possibly unsolvable) problem.

  SPECULATIVE DIRECTION: Could "prismatic cohomology" (Bhatt-Scholze),
  which is designed to unify various p-adic cohomology theories,
  provide a GLOBAL invariant of Z/NZ that doesn't decompose via
  CRT? This is mathematically meaningful as a question but there
  is currently no evidence it would help with factoring.

SECTION 3: WHAT REMAINS POSSIBLE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.1  THE LOGICAL GAP: WHAT HAS NOT BEEN TRIED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

After examining all 20 structural families above (encompassing ~360
specific approaches), we can identify the precise logical gap between
what has been explored and what might be possible.

OBSERVATION 1: EVERY KNOWN APPROACH IS "LOCAL" IN SOME SENSE.

  All explored approaches work with INDIVIDUAL elements of Z/NZ (or
  extensions thereof), computing properties of those elements one at
  a time. Even NFS, which uses algebraic structure (number fields),
  ultimately evaluates a norm polynomial at individual points and
  tests smoothness pointwise.

  The logical gap: GLOBAL properties of Z/NZ as a whole — properties
  that cannot be decomposed into pointwise evaluations — have not
  been systematically explored as a route to factoring.

  Examples of global properties:
  - The SPECTRUM of Z/NZ as a ring (Spec(Z/NZ) = {(p), (q)})
  - The AUTOMORPHISM GROUP of structures over Z/NZ
  - The DEFORMATION THEORY of Z/NZ (how does Z/NZ change as N varies?)
  - GLOBAL cohomological invariants that don't decompose via CRT

  The issue: most global properties either (a) reduce to CRT
  (and hence require knowing p) or (b) are trivial (Spec(Z/NZ) has
  exactly two points, but FINDING them is the problem). The gap is
  whether there exist global properties that are NONTRIVIAL yet
  COMPUTABLE without knowing the decomposition.


OBSERVATION 2: THE "INFORMATION SOURCE" HASN'T CHANGED.

  All known algorithms extract factoring information from the same
  fundamental source: the MULTIPLICATIVE STRUCTURE of Z/NZ, possibly
  lifted to number fields or group varieties.

  The logical gap: are there DIFFERENT sources of information about
  the factorization that are not derived from the multiplicative
  structure?

  Candidate alternative information sources:
  - ADDITIVE structure of Z/NZ interacting with multiplicative
    structure (sum-product phenomena, but Section 2.17 shows
    these are too generic)
  - The RING STRUCTURE of Z/NZ as a whole (not just multiplicative
    or additive separately)
  - DERIVED algebraic structures: K-theory K₀(Z/NZ), Hochschild
    cohomology HH*(Z/NZ), topological Hochschild homology THH(Z/NZ)
  - METRIC or GEOMETRIC properties of Z/NZ viewed as a metric space
    (e.g., under some natural metric induced by the factorization)


OBSERVATION 3: THE "COMBINATION MECHANISM" HASN'T CHANGED.

  All known algorithms combine partial information via LINEAR ALGEBRA
  over GF(2) (or occasionally over Z). The GF(2) bottleneck (B3) is
  a direct consequence.

  The logical gap: are there NONLINEAR combination mechanisms that
  could compose partial information more efficiently?

  Candidate nonlinear mechanisms:
  - Combining relations via group operations in a non-abelian group
  - Using MULTIPLICATIVE combination (products of relations) rather
    than additive (sums of exponent vectors)
  - Tropical algebra: combining relations via (min, +) operations
  - Homological algebra: combining cocycles via cup products or
    higher operations (Massey products, A-infinity structures)


OBSERVATION 4: THE SMOOTHNESS PARADIGM HASN'T BEEN ESCAPED.

  Every known subexponential factoring algorithm relies on finding
  SMOOTH numbers in some structured set. The Dickman function ρ(u)
  governs the cost, and no algebraic trick has changed this.

  The logical gap: is there a way to extract information about p
  from N that does NOT require finding smooth numbers?

  Non-smoothness-based approaches that have been tried:
  - Pollard rho (random walks): O(√p) time, exponential in n/4
  - Fermat's method: finds factors near √N only
  - Lehman's method: O(N^{1/3}) time, exponential

  All of these are polynomial-time in N but exponential in n = log N.
  No known non-smoothness-based approach achieves subexponential time.

  THE DEEP QUESTION: Is smoothness NECESSARY for subexponential
  factoring, or is there an entirely different paradigm?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.2  WHAT MATHEMATICAL OBJECT COULD SATISFY ALL REQUIREMENTS?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Based on the gap analysis above, a barrier-breaking structure would
need to be something genuinely NEW — not a variation of existing
number-theoretic objects. We can, however, characterize its properties
with some precision.

CHARACTERIZATION THEOREM (informal):

  A barrier-breaking structure S(N) must be an object that:

  (P1) Lives in a category C that is NOT a subcategory of:
       - Commutative rings (to avoid CRT decomposition)
       - Abelian groups (to avoid GF(2) bottleneck)
       - Polynomial maps Z^k → Z/NZ (to avoid smoothness bottleneck)

  (P2) Has a "natural" construction from N that does NOT require
       factoring N, yet produces different objects for different
       factorizations.

  (P3) Supports an "evaluation" operation that extracts > O(1) bits
       per evaluation, where the information is about the factoring
       of N (not about some independent random variable).

  (P4) Has algebraic composition that is SUPER-ADDITIVE in
       information: k evaluations composed yield ω(k) bits.

  (P5) Is constructible in time L[α] for some α < 1/3.

Let us examine what P1-P5 allow:

CANDIDATE TYPE A: NON-COMMUTATIVE ALGEBRAIC STRUCTURES OVER Z/NZ

  If S(N) is a non-commutative ring or group derived from Z/NZ,
  it might avoid the CRT decomposition (which is a phenomenon of
  COMMUTATIVE algebra). For example:

  - Matrix rings M_n(Z/NZ): these DO decompose via CRT
    (M_n(Z/NZ) ≅ M_n(Z/pZ) × M_n(Z/qZ)), so they don't help.

  - Free algebras or group algebras: Z/NZ[G] for non-abelian G.
    These decompose as Z/pZ[G] × Z/qZ[G], so CRT still applies.

  - Non-associative algebras: octonions O(Z/NZ), Jordan algebras,
    Lie algebras over Z/NZ. These are less studied and the CRT
    decomposition is less straightforward. HOWEVER, the Lie
    algebra g(Z/NZ) ≅ g(Z/pZ) × g(Z/qZ) still holds for most
    natural constructions.

  VERDICT: Non-commutativity alone does NOT escape CRT. The CRT
  decomposition is a phenomenon of the BASE RING Z/NZ, not of
  structures over it. Any structure OVER Z/NZ inherits CRT.

CANDIDATE TYPE B: STRUCTURES NOT "OVER" Z/NZ BUT "DERIVED FROM" Z/NZ

  The key distinction: a structure "over Z/NZ" (like a module or
  algebra with Z/NZ as base ring) automatically inherits CRT.
  But a structure "derived from" Z/NZ (like a topological space
  or graph CONSTRUCTED from Z/NZ) might not.

  Examples:
  - The CAYLEY GRAPH of (Z/NZ)* under specific generators:
    this is a graph, not a Z/NZ-module, so it doesn't directly
    inherit CRT. But its spectrum does decompose (Section 2.14).

  - The UNIT GROUP LATTICE: view (Z/NZ)* as an abelian group
    and study its subgroup lattice. This lattice is
    Sub((Z/pZ)* × (Z/qZ)*), which has more structure than
    Sub((Z/pZ)*) × Sub((Z/qZ)*) — the subgroup lattice does
    NOT decompose as a product! This is because subgroups of a
    product A × B include "diagonal" subgroups that project
    non-trivially onto both factors.

    However: enumerating subgroups of (Z/NZ)* requires knowing
    the group structure, which requires knowing p and q (since
    (Z/NZ)* ≅ (Z/pZ)* × (Z/qZ)* = Z/(p-1)Z × Z/(q-1)Z).

  - HIGHER K-THEORY of Z/NZ: K₀(Z/NZ) = Z² (reflecting two
    indecomposable projective modules), K₁(Z/NZ) = (Z/NZ)*.
    Higher K-groups K_n(Z/NZ) for n ≥ 2 might carry non-CRT
    information, but computing them is extremely hard (equivalent
    to computing stable homotopy groups, which is a major open
    problem in algebraic topology even for simple rings).

CANDIDATE TYPE C: DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS OVER Z/NZ

  Consider a dynamical system f: Z/NZ → Z/NZ (e.g., x → x² + c).
  The dynamics of f do NOT necessarily decompose via CRT in a
  simple way. The orbit structure of f on Z/NZ is related to but
  not identical to the orbit structures of f on Z/pZ and Z/qZ.

  Specifically: if O_N(x₀) is the orbit of x₀ under f in Z/NZ,
  and O_p(x₀ mod p), O_q(x₀ mod q) are the orbits in Z/pZ, Z/qZ,
  then:
    |O_N(x₀)| = lcm(|O_p(x₀ mod p)|, |O_q(x₀ mod q)|)

  This lcm structure is LESS opaque than full CRT: it constrains
  |O_p| and |O_q| to be divisors of |O_N|. Pollard's rho method
  exploits this (via birthday collisions in orbits), but only
  achieves O(√p) time.

  THE QUESTION: Is there a family of dynamical systems f_N where
  the orbit structure reveals MORE than O(1) bits about p per
  orbit computed, yet orbits are computable in poly(log N) time?

  CURRENT STATUS: Unknown, but the lcm structure of orbits over
  Z/NZ is genuinely more tractable than full CRT opacity, making
  this a non-trivially open direction.

CANDIDATE TYPE D: ENTROPY/INFORMATION-THEORETIC OBJECTS

  Consider the DISTRIBUTION of some function f(a) as a ranges over
  Z/NZ (or a structured subset). The distribution D_N of f over
  Z/NZ is related to but different from the product distribution
  D_p × D_q (the distribution of (f(a mod p), f(a mod q))).

  For example: the distribution of Jacobi symbols
  (a/N) = (a/p)(a/q) over random a carries exactly 1 bit about
  the factorization (distinguishing quadratic residues from
  non-residues with Jacobi symbol 1).

  THE QUESTION: Is there a function f and a statistical test T
  such that:
  - T(D_N) reveals > 1 bit about the factorization per sample
  - Each sample is computable in poly(log N) time
  - The test T is computable in poly(k) time given k samples

  This is essentially asking: can we AMPLIFY the 1-bit information
  from quadratic residuosity to multi-bit information without
  increasing the per-sample cost proportionally?

  KNOWN BARRIER: Higher-order residuosity (e.g., k-th power
  residuosity for k > 2) requires knowing the group structure of
  (Z/NZ)*, which requires knowing p and q when k ∤ (p-1)(q-1).
  So higher residuosity is CRT-opaque.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.3  WHAT A NEGATIVE RESULT (PROOF OF L[1/3] OPTIMALITY) WOULD
     LOOK LIKE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A proof that no classical algorithm can factor N in time o(L[1/3])
would be one of the most important results in computational complexity.
Let us characterize what such a proof would need to establish.

APPROACH 1: ORACLE/BLACK-BOX LOWER BOUND

  Prove that any algorithm with oracle access to Z/NZ (i.e., that
  can perform ring operations and equality tests in Z/NZ but has
  no "special knowledge" of how Z/NZ is represented) requires
  Ω(L[1/3]) ring operations to factor N.

  CURRENT STATUS: Shoup (1997) proved that any GENERIC RING
  ALGORITHM for factoring requires Ω(N^{1/4}) ring operations.
  This is a lower bound of 2^{Ω(n)} — exponential, but weaker
  than L[1/3] for large N.

  DIFFICULTY: Generic ring lower bounds cannot capture algorithms
  that exploit the specific REPRESENTATION of elements (as integers
  0, 1, ..., N-1) rather than just the ring structure. NFS exploits
  representation (via smoothness of integer representatives).

APPROACH 2: INFORMATION-THEORETIC LOWER BOUND

  Prove that any algorithm that extracts information about p from
  Z/NZ must pay at least L[1/3] computational cost per bit of
  information.

  This would require formalizing:
  (a) What "extracting information about p" means computationally
  (b) A lower bound on the computational cost per bit
  (c) That n/2 bits are necessary (which is trivially true)

  DIFFICULTY: Part (b) is essentially a circuit complexity lower
  bound, and no super-linear circuit lower bounds are known for
  explicit functions (the fundamental barrier in complexity theory).

APPROACH 3: REDUCTION TO A KNOWN HARD PROBLEM

  Prove that factoring N in time o(L[1/3]) would imply breaking
  some other assumption believed to be hard (e.g., solving
  worst-case lattice problems, or violating a well-established
  conjecture).

  CURRENT STATUS: No such reduction is known. In fact, the
  relationship between factoring and other hard problems (LWE,
  lattice problems, etc.) is poorly understood.

APPROACH 4: SMOOTHNESS BARRIER FORMALIZATION

  Prove that any factoring algorithm must, implicitly or explicitly,
  find smooth numbers in a set of size ≥ L[1/3], and that the
  smoothness probability in any such set is ≤ L[-1/3].

  This would need:
  (a) A theorem that smooth numbers are NECESSARY (not just
      sufficient) for subexponential factoring
  (b) A universal smoothness probability bound for all "factoring-
      relevant" sets

  DIFFICULTY: Part (a) is wide open. Pollard's rho does not use
  smooth numbers and achieves O(√p) time. It is conceivable that
  a non-smoothness-based approach could achieve subexponential time,
  which would refute (a).

APPROACH 5: BARRIERS ON BARRIERS (META-BARRIERS)

  Prove that current proof techniques CANNOT prove L[1/3] optimality.
  This would be analogous to the Baker-Gill-Solovay theorem (oracle
  separations show relativization cannot resolve P vs NP) or the
  Razborov-Rudich natural proofs barrier.

  CURRENT STATUS: Boneh-Lipton (1996) showed that certain oracle
  techniques cannot prove super-polynomial lower bounds for
  factoring. This suggests that proving L[1/3] optimality will
  require fundamentally new proof techniques, beyond oracles and
  generic models.

THE MOST LIKELY FORM OF A NEGATIVE RESULT:

  Based on the current state of complexity theory, a proof of
  L[1/3] optimality would most likely come from:

  (i)  A NEW computational model that captures the essential features
       of factoring algorithms more precisely than generic ring
       algorithms (perhaps "smooth-number algorithms" or
       "relation-finding algorithms" as a formal class).

  (ii) A CONDITIONAL result: "L[1/3] is optimal among algorithms
       that [use smooth numbers / find relations / work in number
       fields / ...]," i.e., a barrier result CONDITIONAL on the
       algorithmic paradigm.

  (iii) An UNCONDITIONAL lower bound using a new proof technique that
        bypasses the relativization and natural proofs barriers.
        This would be a major breakthrough in complexity theory
        independent of its implications for factoring.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3.4  THE FUNDAMENTAL TENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We can now precisely state the fundamental tension that any
barrier-breaking approach must resolve:

TENSION 1: COMPUTABILITY VS. INFORMATION

  To carry information about p, a structure must be SENSITIVE to p.
  But p is hidden behind CRT, so sensitivity requires "seeing
  through" CRT. Yet any structure that sees through CRT can be
  used to COMPUTE p, meaning the structure itself encodes a
  factoring algorithm. The tension: the structure must encode
  information about p WITHOUT encoding p explicitly — it must
  carry PARTIAL information that can be efficiently AMPLIFIED.

TENSION 2: EFFICIENCY VS. GENERALITY

  An efficient structure (polynomial-time constructible) must be
  algebraically "simple" (bounded degree, bounded dimension).
  But simple algebraic structures over Z/NZ decompose via CRT.
  The tension: the structure must be simple enough to construct
  efficiently, yet complex enough to resist CRT decomposition.

TENSION 3: ALGEBRAIC VS. ANALYTIC

  Algebraic information (group orders, class numbers, Frobenius
  eigenvalues) is exact but hard to compute. Analytic information
  (L-function values, density estimates, probabilistic bounds)
  is approximate but easier to access. The tension: the structure
  must bridge the algebraic-analytic divide, extracting exact
  factoring information from approximate computations at sub-L[1/3]
  cost.

RESOLUTION OF TENSIONS (IF POSSIBLE):

  A structure that resolves all three tensions would likely be:

  - GLOBAL rather than local (avoids pointwise CRT decomposition)
  - ALGEBRAIC-ANALYTIC hybrid (combines exact and approximate info)
  - NON-FUNCTORIAL from Z/NZ (avoids automatic CRT decomposition)
  - COMPOSABLE via non-commutative or non-linear operations
    (avoids GF(2) bottleneck)
  - CONNECTED to smoothness but not DEPENDENT on it (uses smooth
    numbers as auxiliary tools, not as the primary information
    source)

  Whether such a structure exists is the fundamental open question
  of factoring complexity.

SECTION 4: RECOMMENDED RESEARCH DIRECTIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Based on the analysis in Sections 1-3, we prioritize research
directions by three criteria:
  (a) NOVELTY: How far is this from well-explored territory?
  (b) FALSIFIABILITY: Can we quickly test whether the approach
      has any chance of working?
  (c) POTENTIAL IMPACT: If successful, would it break L[1/3] or
      just improve the constant?

Directions are listed in decreasing order of overall priority.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.1  [PRIORITY: HIGHEST]
     DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS AND ORBIT COMPLEXITY OVER Z/NZ
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★★★ (barely explored beyond Pollard rho)
Falsifiability: ★★★★☆ (concrete computational experiments possible)
Potential impact: ★★★☆☆ (might improve exponent; unclear if below 1/3)

THESIS: The orbit structure of polynomial dynamical systems
f: Z/NZ → Z/NZ contains more information about the factorization
than has been extracted by existing algorithms.

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. For f(x) = x² + c and random c, study the FULL orbit graph
     Γ_f(N) (the functional graph of f on Z/NZ). This graph has
     connected components, cycle lengths, and tail lengths that
     encode information about p and q through the lcm structure:

       cycle_length_N = lcm(cycle_length_p, cycle_length_q)

  2. QUESTION: Can the DISTRIBUTION of cycle lengths and tail
     lengths in Γ_f(N) — over many random choices of c — reveal
     more than O(1) bits about p per dynamical system?

  3. QUANTITATIVE TARGET: If the cycle length distribution reveals
     Ω(log log N) bits per system, and each system can be partially
     analyzed in poly(log N) time, then O(n / log log n) systems
     suffice, giving total time O(n · poly(n) / log log n) — still
     not subexponential, but this would be a NEW paradigm different
     from smooth-number methods.

  4. DEEPER DIRECTION: Consider HIGHER-DIMENSIONAL dynamical systems
     F: (Z/NZ)^k → (Z/NZ)^k. The orbit structure in dimension k
     might reveal exponentially more information per orbit as k grows.
     The cost per orbit step grows as poly(k), so the tradeoff
     between information and cost is the key question.

  5. CONNECTION TO ARITHMETIC DYNAMICS: The Mandelbrot set over
     finite fields has been studied (Bridy, Jones, et al.), and
     there are connections to Galois theory and algebraic number
     theory. These connections are UNEXPLORED for factoring.

WHY THIS DIRECTION IS PROMISING:
  - Dynamical systems over Z/NZ have a lcm structure that is
    genuinely more tractable than full CRT opacity.
  - The functional graph Γ_f(N) is a NON-ALGEBRAIC structure
    derived from Z/NZ, so it doesn't automatically inherit CRT.
  - Computational experiments can be done immediately and
    inexpensively.

WHY IT MIGHT FAIL:
  - Orbit lengths are typically O(√N) = O(2^{n/2}), which is
    exponential. Extracting information from PARTIAL orbits
    (without computing the full cycle) is the challenge.
  - The lcm structure might not provide more than O(1) bits
    without computing full orbits.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.2  [PRIORITY: HIGH]
     DERIVED ALGEBRAIC INVARIANTS: K-THEORY, THH, AND TC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★★★ (essentially unexplored for factoring)
Falsifiability: ★★☆☆☆ (requires heavy mathematical machinery)
Potential impact: ★★★★★ (could be paradigm-breaking if any invariant works)

THESIS: Higher algebraic K-theory and topological Hochschild homology
of Z/NZ might carry factoring information that is not CRT-decomposable.

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. COMPUTE K_n(Z/NZ) for small n (n = 0, 1, 2, 3):
     - K₀(Z/NZ) ≅ Z² (known: two indecomposable projectives)
     - K₁(Z/NZ) ≅ (Z/NZ)* (known: the units)
     - K₂(Z/NZ) = ? (not known in closed form for general N)
     - K₃(Z/NZ) = ? (deeply connected to algebraic topology)

  2. QUESTION: Does K₂(Z/NZ) carry information beyond what is in
     K₀ and K₁? Specifically, is the natural map
       K₂(Z/NZ) → K₂(Z/pZ) × K₂(Z/qZ)
     an isomorphism, or does it have a nontrivial kernel/cokernel?

     If the kernel is nontrivial, it would be a "global" invariant
     of Z/NZ that does NOT decompose via CRT — exactly what we need.

  3. TOPOLOGICAL HOCHSCHILD HOMOLOGY (THH):
     THH(Z/NZ) is a spectrum (in the sense of stable homotopy theory)
     whose homotopy groups π_*(THH(Z/NZ)) might carry non-CRT
     information. The Bökstedt spectral sequence relates THH to
     Hochschild homology, which is more computable.

  4. TOPOLOGICAL CYCLIC HOMOLOGY (TC):
     TC(Z/NZ) → K(Z/NZ) is an approximation to K-theory that is
     sometimes MORE computable. If TC(Z/NZ) carries non-CRT
     information and is computable in sub-L[1/3] time, this would
     be a breakthrough.

WHY THIS DIRECTION IS PROMISING:
  - K-theory is specifically designed to capture "global" algebraic
    invariants that go beyond local/CRT decomposition.
  - The exact sequences in K-theory (Mayer-Vietoris, localization)
    relate K(Z/NZ) to K(Z) and K(Z/pZ) in non-trivial ways that
    might be exploitable.
  - THH and TC are computable (in principle) and have been
    computed for simple rings.

WHY IT MIGHT FAIL:
  - K-theory of Z/NZ very likely DOES decompose via CRT at all
    levels: K_n(R × S) ≅ K_n(R) × K_n(S) for product rings.
    Since Z/NZ ≅ Z/pZ × Z/qZ (as rings via CRT), we get
    K_n(Z/NZ) ≅ K_n(Z/pZ) × K_n(Z/qZ). This is an immediate
    consequence of the Morita invariance and product formula in
    K-theory.

  COUNTER-ARGUMENT: The isomorphism Z/NZ ≅ Z/pZ × Z/qZ requires
  KNOWING p and q to be EXPLICIT. K_n(Z/NZ) can be computed
  (in principle) from Z/NZ alone, while K_n(Z/pZ) × K_n(Z/qZ)
  cannot. So while the abstract groups are isomorphic, COMPUTING
  the isomorphism IS the factoring problem. The question is
  whether the COMPUTATION of K_n reveals the factorization as
  a byproduct.

  REALITY CHECK: This is likely to hit the same barrier as class
  groups (Section 2.5) — the invariant CONTAINS the factorization
  but COMPUTING it costs ≥ L[1/3]. However, this has not been
  formally established for all K-theoretic invariants.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.3  [PRIORITY: HIGH]
     NON-SMOOTHNESS SUBEXPONENTIAL PARADIGMS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★★☆ (conceptually clear but technically unexplored)
Falsifiability: ★★★★★ (new algorithm or impossibility proof)
Potential impact: ★★★★★ (breaks the smoothness paradigm entirely)

THESIS: Is there a subexponential factoring algorithm that does NOT
rely on finding smooth numbers?

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. FORMALIZE the class of "smooth-number algorithms" as a
     computational model. Define precisely what it means for an
     algorithm to "rely on smooth numbers" (e.g., the algorithm
     must at some point produce integers that are tested for
     smoothness, and the algorithm's running time depends on
     the smoothness probability via the Dickman function).

  2. PROVE that L[1/3] is optimal within this formal class.
     This would be a CONDITIONAL lower bound: "among smooth-number
     algorithms, L[1/3] is optimal." This is likely provable with
     current techniques and would be a significant result.

  3. EXPLORE non-smooth paradigms:
     (a) REPRESENTATION-THEORETIC: Can factoring be reduced to
         finding a specific representation of a group related to N?
     (b) GEOMETRIC: Can factoring be reduced to finding a specific
         point on a variety defined by N?
     (c) TOPOLOGICAL: Can factoring be reduced to computing a
         topological invariant (genus, fundamental group, homology)
         of a space derived from N?
     (d) DYNAMICAL: Can factoring be reduced to computing properties
         of a dynamical system derived from N? (overlap with 4.1)

  4. Each non-smooth paradigm should be ANALYZED: what is the
     computational cost of the relevant operation, and how much
     factoring information does it yield?

WHY THIS DIRECTION IS PROMISING:
  - The smooth-number paradigm has been the ONLY subexponential
    approach for 50+ years. Proving it optimal (within its class)
    would be a landmark result. Finding a non-smooth alternative
    would be even more significant.
  - The formal class of smooth-number algorithms is likely
    definable and analyzable with current mathematical tools.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.4  [PRIORITY: MEDIUM-HIGH]
     GLOBAL-TO-LOCAL: THE SPECTRAL APPROACH TO Z/NZ
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★☆☆ (spectral methods are known, but specific applications aren't)
Falsifiability: ★★★★☆ (concrete computations possible)
Potential impact: ★★★☆☆ (might improve constant, unlikely to change exponent)

THESIS: The SPECTRAL THEORY of operators on L²(Z/NZ) or related
spaces might provide factoring information that is not captured by
pointwise evaluation.

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. Consider the CONVOLUTION OPERATOR T_f : L²(Z/NZ) → L²(Z/NZ)
     defined by T_f(g) = f * g for some fixed function f.
     The eigenvalues of T_f are the DFT coefficients of f, and
     these carry information about the additive structure of Z/NZ.

  2. Now consider MULTIPLICATIVE convolution:
     M_f(g)(x) = Σ_{yz=x} f(y)g(z)
     This operator's spectrum reflects the MULTIPLICATIVE structure.

  3. QUESTION: Can the JOINT spectrum of additive and multiplicative
     convolution operators on L²(Z/NZ) reveal the factorization?

     This is related to the sum-product phenomenon (Section 2.17)
     but in a more structured (spectral) form.

  4. UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE APPROACH: The additive and multiplicative
     Fourier transforms on Z/NZ satisfy uncertainty principles.
     These uncertainty principles are DIFFERENT for Z/NZ than for
     Z/pZ (because Z/NZ has zero divisors). Can this difference
     be exploited?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.5  [PRIORITY: MEDIUM]
     PRISMATIC AND MOTIVIC APPROACHES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★★★ (cutting-edge mathematics, untouched for factoring)
Falsifiability: ★☆☆☆☆ (very difficult to compute anything)
Potential impact: ★★★★★ (if it works, completely new paradigm)

THESIS: Prismatic cohomology (Bhatt-Scholze) provides a UNIFIED
framework for p-adic cohomology theories. Could there be a
"prismatic" invariant of Z/NZ that does not require choosing a prime?

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. QUESTION: Can the prismatic site of Spec(Z/NZ) be defined
     and computed without knowing p and q? The prismatic site is
     built from prisms (δ-rings), and prisms over Z/NZ might have
     a richer structure than the simple CRT decomposition.

  2. ABSOLUTE PRISMATIC COHOMOLOGY: The absolute prismatic site
     (Bhatt-Lurie) is defined intrinsically, without choosing a
     base prime. Its cohomology groups for Spec(Z/NZ) might carry
     factoring information.

  3. MOTIVIC COHOMOLOGY OF Z/NZ: Are there motivic invariants
     (e.g., motivic homotopy groups π_{p,q}^{mot}(Spec(Z/NZ)))
     that encode the factorization in a computationally accessible
     way?

WHY THIS DIRECTION IS SPECULATIVE BUT IMPORTANT:
  - Prismatic cohomology is specifically designed to handle the
    "choose a prime" problem in a unified way — exactly the
    problem that p-adic methods face in factoring.
  - If ANY cohomological invariant of Z/NZ avoids CRT decomposition,
    the prismatic framework is the most likely place to find it.
  - This is a 50-year horizon direction: the mathematics is still
    being developed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4.6  [PRIORITY: MEDIUM]
     ENTROPY AND INFORMATION-THEORETIC FACTORING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★★☆ (information theory rarely applied to factoring)
Falsifiability: ★★★★★ (concrete bounds can be computed)
Potential impact: ★★★☆☆ (likely lower bounds rather than algorithms)

THESIS: Apply information-theoretic tools (entropy, mutual information,
channel capacity) to PRECISELY quantify the per-operation information
yield of factoring algorithms.

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. MODEL the factoring problem as a CHANNEL: the "channel" is
     Z/NZ, the "input" is an operation we perform, and the "output"
     is the result. The "message" is p. What is the CAPACITY of
     this channel?

  2. For specific operations (modular arithmetic, exponentiation,
     smoothness testing), COMPUTE the mutual information:
       I(output; p | N, operation)

  3. DERIVE a general bound: for ANY operation computable in time T,
     the mutual information about p is at most f(T) bits, where f
     is some function.

  4. PROVE (or disprove): f(T) = O(T / L[1/3]) for T ≤ L[1/3].
     This would show that L[1/3] operations are necessary to
     extract n/2 bits.

4.7  [PRIORITY: MEDIUM-LOW]
     QUANTUM-CLASSICAL HYBRID APPROACHES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★☆☆☆ (active research area)
Falsifiability: ★★★☆☆ (requires quantum hardware or strong simulation)
Potential impact: ★★★★☆ (reduce quantum resources for factoring)

THESIS: Could a small quantum computation (poly(log N) qubits) provide
a "boost" that helps a classical algorithm beat L[1/3]?

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. Regev's algorithm (2023) uses O(√n) qubits and O(√n) quantum

     post-processing via lattice reduction. This shows quantum-
     classical hybrids can factor N.

  2. QUESTION: What is the minimum quantum resource (qubits, gates,
     depth) needed to provide a "quantum advantage" for factoring
     beyond L[1/3]?

  3. Can SHALLOW quantum circuits (constant or logarithmic depth)
     provide any information about p that is inaccessible classically?

  4. This connects to the question of whether BQP ≠ BPP restricted
     to the factoring problem.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Novelty: ★★★☆☆ (long-standing open problem)
Falsifiability: ★★★★★ (a proof is a proof)
Potential impact: ★★★★★ (resolves the question definitively)

THESIS: Prove conditional or unconditional lower bounds for factoring

SPECIFIC RESEARCH PROGRAM:

  1. Define a RICHER computational model that captures the key

  2. Prove that factoring requires super-polynomial time in the

  3. Connect factoring lower bounds to well-studied complexity

SECTION 5: SYNTHESIS AND CONCLUSIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.1  THE META-THEOREM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

After analyzing ~360 approaches across 20 structural families, we can
state the following informal meta-theorem:

META-THEOREM (informal):
  Any classical factoring algorithm using:
    (i)   algebraic structures "over" Z/NZ (modules, algebras, varieties)
    (ii)  smooth number detection as the information extraction mechanism
    (iii) GF(2) linear algebra as the combination mechanism
  has running time ≥ L[1/3, c] for some constant c > 0.

  This meta-theorem is NOT proven, but every known algorithm falls
  within its scope, and the three conditions correspond precisely
  to the three deepest barriers (CRT opacity, smoothness bottleneck,
  GF(2) bottleneck).

COROLLARY: To break L[1/3], an algorithm must violate at least one
of conditions (i), (ii), or (iii).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.2  THE FIVE-REQUIREMENT SPECIFICATION (SUMMARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A barrier-breaking mathematical structure S(N) must satisfy:

  R1. COMPUTABILITY: Constructible from N in poly(n) space, L[α] time
      for α < 1/3.

  R2. INFORMATION DENSITY: Each evaluation yields > O(1) bits about p.

  R3. COMPOSABILITY: Evaluations compose algebraically with
      super-additive information gain.

  R4. CRT-OPACITY AVOIDANCE: Construction and composition do not
      require knowing p.

  R5. SUB-L[1/3] CONSTRUCTIBILITY: All operations cost < L[1/3].

  Additionally, S(N) should be:

  P1. NOT a structure "over" Z/NZ (to avoid automatic CRT decomposition).

  P2. NOT dependent on smooth numbers (to avoid the Dickman function
      bottleneck).

  P3. NOT combining information via GF(2) (to avoid the linear
      algebra bottleneck).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.3  THE HONEST ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

After this comprehensive analysis, the honest assessment is:

1. THE L[1/3] BARRIER IS VERY LIKELY REAL for all "natural" factoring
   algorithms. The convergence of all 360 approaches to the same
   exponent, from vastly different mathematical starting points, is
   strong evidence that L[1/3] captures a deep truth about the
   computational complexity of factoring over Z.

2. THE BARRIER IS NOT PROVEN. There is a genuine logical gap between
   "every known algorithm achieves L[1/3]" and "every possible
   algorithm achieves L[1/3]." This gap might be closeable
   (proving L[1/3] optimality) or exploitable (finding a better
   algorithm).

3. THE MOST PROMISING DIRECTIONS are those that attack conditions
   (i)-(iii) of the meta-theorem:
   - Dynamical systems (attack condition (i): not "over" Z/NZ)
   - Non-smoothness paradigms (attack condition (ii))
   - Derived algebraic invariants (attack condition (i) and (iii))

4. THE HARDEST BARRIER IS CRT OPACITY (R4). This barrier is not
   merely a consequence of current techniques — it reflects the
   fundamental algebraic structure of Z/NZ as a product ring.
   Breaking this barrier likely requires mathematical objects
   that do not yet exist in our current algebraic toolkit.

5. THE FUNCTION FIELD ANALOGY provides both hope and despair:
   - HOPE: Factoring is polynomial-time over F_q[T], showing that
     L[1/3] is not information-theoretically necessary.
   - DESPAIR: The reason factoring is easy over F_q[T] (Frobenius)
     has no Z-analogue, and the "rigidity" of Z versus F_q[T] is
     a fundamental number-theoretic phenomenon that may be
     impassable by any algorithmic technique.

6. A PROOF OF L[1/3] OPTIMALITY would be a major achievement in
   complexity theory, likely requiring new proof techniques beyond
   current oracle/generic methods. This might be approachable via
   conditional results (optimality within specific algorithm classes)
   rather than unconditional results.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.4  FINAL CHARACTERIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The barrier-breaking object, if it exists, would be a mathematical
structure that:

  "Extracts global, non-CRT-decomposable information about Z/NZ
   at super-constant rate per evaluation, using a non-smooth,
   non-GF(2) composition mechanism, all constructible in time
   o(L[1/3])."

In the language of the five barriers:

  It breaks B1 (L[1/3] wall) by finding information FASTER.
  It bridges B2 (archimedean/non-archimedean gap) by using neither.
  It avoids B3 (GF(2) bottleneck) by composing non-linearly.
  It penetrates B4 (CRT opacity) by being non-functorial from Z/NZ.
  It overcomes B5 (Z-rigidity) by accessing structure Z doesn't
    obviously have.

No known mathematical object satisfies all five. Whether one EXISTS
is, in our assessment, the deepest open question in algorithmic
number theory — and one whose resolution, in either direction, would
transform our understanding of the boundary between algebra,
analysis, and computation.
