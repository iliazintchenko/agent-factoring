DEFINITIVE TOP 5 THEORETICAL INSIGHTS ON INTEGER FACTORIZATION
================================================================
Distilled from ~130 approaches across smoothness-based, group-order,
algebraic-structure, information-theoretic, computational-complexity,
and exotic paradigms.


#1. THE L[1/3] BARRIER IS STRUCTURAL, NOT ACCIDENTAL
=====================================================
The alpha = 1/(k+1) formula governs all known subexponential methods:
trial division (k=0, alpha=1), Pollard/QS-class (k=1, alpha=1/2),
NFS-class (k=2, alpha=1/3). Each generation exploits one additional
layer of algebraic structure (integers → polynomial rings → number
fields). Reaching L[1/4] would require a third independent algebraic
extension — but number fields already exhaust the available structure
over Q. There is no known "third ring" to adjoin. The barrier is not
that we lack clever algorithms; it is that the algebraic hierarchy
over Z terminates at depth k=2 for smoothness exploitation. Every
approach we tested that appeared to break L[1/3] either smuggled in
an assumption equivalent to factoring or collapsed back to L[1/3]
when the hidden costs were properly accounted.


#2. THE ARCHIMEDEAN / NON-ARCHIMEDEAN SIZE GAP IS INESCAPABLE
=============================================================
N = p·q encodes its factors simultaneously in two metric worlds:
archimedean (real/complex size: |N|, |p|, |q|) and non-archimedean
(p-adic divisibility structure). Any factoring algorithm must
reconcile information from both. The core obstruction: archimedean
methods (lattice reduction, continued fractions, analytic estimates)
see N as a point on a line and can localize factors to intervals of
width ~√N — but no finer, because p and q are metrically
indistinguishable at that scale. Non-archimedean methods (p-adic
lifting, Hensel's lemma, local-global approaches) can refine
arbitrarily at one prime but require already knowing which prime —
a circular dependency. Approaches that try to combine both
(adelic methods, Hasse-Minkowski style) hit the product formula:
gains at one place are exactly offset by losses at others. This is
why lattice methods plateau at √N and why no local-to-global
strategy has broken through: the size gap between what each metric
world reveals is precisely the information content of the factors.


#3. THE 1-BIT-PER-RELATION GF(2) BOTTLENECK
============================================
Every relation-based factoring algorithm (QS, NFS, and all variants)
ultimately reduces to finding a dependency in a matrix over GF(2).
Each smooth relation contributes exactly 1 bit of constraint toward
the ~π(B) bits needed (where B is the smoothness bound). This is
NOT an implementation detail — it is an information-theoretic floor.
A relation a² ≡ b² (mod N) compresses the multiplicative structure
of Z/NZ into a single binary vector recording exponent parities.
All the "real-valued" information in the relation (the actual sizes
of a, b, their smooth factorizations) is discarded. No known method
extracts more than 1 usable bit per relation because the congruence-
of-squares framework inherently binarizes. Breaking this bottleneck
would require a fundamentally different endgame — not a congruence
of squares but some richer algebraic coincidence. We found none that
survives analysis: every candidate (higher-residue symbols, K-theory
classes, Brauer group elements) either reduces back to GF(2) parity
or requires exponentially many instances of its own.


#4. CRT OPACITY: MODULAR COMPOSITION DESTROYS ACCESSIBLE STRUCTURE
==================================================================
The Chinese Remainder Theorem guarantees that Z/NZ ≅ Z/pZ × Z/qZ,
so "in principle" the factor structure is present in every computation
mod N. But CRT composition is a one-way opacity barrier for all
known algorithmic paradigms. Specifically:

  - Additive structure: addition mod N is trivially accessible, but
    carries no factor information (Z/NZ is cyclic either way).
  - Multiplicative structure: (Z/NZ)* ≅ (Z/pZ)* × (Z/qZ)*, but
    the group order φ(N) is as hard to compute as factoring.
  - Ring structure: the idempotents (1,0) and (0,1) in the product
    decomposition correspond exactly to the factors, but finding
    nontrivial idempotents in Z/NZ IS factoring.
  - Spectral structure: eigenvalues of Cayley graphs, zeta functions
    of Z/NZ — all encode factors but only via quantities that are
    themselves as hard to compute as factoring.

CRT opacity means: the factors are "right there" in every algebraic
object associated to Z/NZ, but every known route to extracting them
passes through a computation equivalent to factoring itself. This is
the deepest form of circularity we encountered, and it defeated every
"structural" approach (spectral, cohomological, K-theoretic, motivic).


#5. Z HAS NO EXPLOITABLE ENDOMORPHISMS (THE RIGIDITY PRINCIPLE)
================================================================
The integers Z, as a ring, have trivial endomorphism ring: the only
ring endomorphism Z → Z is the identity. This seemingly elementary
fact has profound algorithmic consequences. Contrast with elliptic
curves, where complex multiplication provides extra endomorphisms
that enable dramatic speedups (CM method, Schoof's algorithm). Or
with function fields over finite fields (where Frobenius is an
endomorphism enabling polynomial-time factoring of the analogous
objects). The reason factoring integers is hard while factoring
polynomials over F_q is easy is precisely this: F_q[x] has Frobenius;
Z does not. Every "transport" strategy we investigated — mapping
factoring to a richer algebraic setting (elliptic curves, abelian
varieties, motives, derived categories) — ultimately fails because
the map must factor through Z, inheriting its rigidity. You cannot
"borrow" endomorphisms from a richer structure without first solving
a problem equivalent to factoring. This rigidity is also why quantum
computing helps: Shor's algorithm works by synthetically creating an
endomorphism (the quantum Fourier transform over Z/NZ) that Z does
not naturally possess, exploiting quantum superposition to bypass
the classical rigidity constraint.


SYNTHESIS
=========
These five insights are deeply interrelated:

  #1 (L[1/3] barrier) is the QUANTITATIVE manifestation.
  #2 (size gap) is the METRIC obstruction.
  #3 (GF(2) bottleneck) is the INFORMATION-THEORETIC floor.
  #4 (CRT opacity) is the ALGEBRAIC circularity.
  #5 (no endomorphism) is the STRUCTURAL root cause.

Read together, they say: factoring is hard because Z is rigid (#5),
which makes CRT composition opaque (#4), which forces algorithms
into a binarized search (#3), which cannot be shortcut metrically
(#2), which manifests as the L[1/3] complexity wall (#1).

No classical approach among the ~130 investigated escapes all five
constraints simultaneously. Any breakthrough would need to violate
at least one — most likely #5, by finding a non-obvious "effective
endomorphism" of some structure faithfully encoding Z/NZ, or #3, by
discovering a non-binary algebraic coincidence that leaks more than
1 bit per relation. Both appear to require fundamentally new
mathematics, not merely cleverer algorithms.
