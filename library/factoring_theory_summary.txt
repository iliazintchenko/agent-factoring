THE FACTORING LANDSCAPE: A COMPREHENSIVE THEORETICAL SURVEY
============================================================

Based on systematic investigation of ~75 approaches to integer factorization,
this document synthesizes the structural reasons why factoring is hard, what
the known methods achieve, why they plateau at L[1/3], and what would be
required to break through.

Throughout, we use the standard complexity notation:

    L_N[α, c] = exp(c · (ln N)^α · (ln ln N)^(1-α))

where α ∈ [0, 1] interpolates between polynomial (α = 0) and exponential
(α = 1), and c is the leading constant.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. THE FACTORING PROBLEM: WHAT WE WANT, WHY IT MATTERS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Given N = p · q (product of two unknown primes of roughly equal size), find
p and q. This is the integer factorization problem for semiprimes.

Why it matters:
- RSA cryptography (key sizes 2048–4096 bits) relies on factoring being hard.
- Factoring is known to be in NP ∩ co-NP, so it is unlikely to be NP-complete.
- It is not known to be in P (classically). Shor's algorithm places it in BQP.
- The problem has a unique structural character: the answer is determined by
  a single bit-string (the factorization), yet extracting that bit-string
  from the product appears to require sub-exponential but super-polynomial
  work.

The practical question: can we factor N in time better than L_N[1/3, c]
using a classical (non-quantum) algorithm?


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
2. THE THREE CLASSICAL FAMILIES OF FACTORING ALGORITHMS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Every known factoring algorithm falls into one of three families, each with
a characteristic complexity profile and a characteristic bottleneck.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2A. SMOOTHNESS-BASED METHODS: L[1/2] → L[1/3]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Core idea: Find x, y such that x² ≡ y² (mod N), x ≢ ±y (mod N), then
gcd(x - y, N) is a nontrivial factor. To do this, collect "relations" —
integers that are smooth (all prime factors below a bound B) — and combine
them via linear algebra over GF(2) to construct the desired square.

The fundamental tradeoff: larger smoothness bound B means each individual
number is more likely to be smooth (easier to find relations), but the
linear algebra system has more columns (harder to solve). Optimal B
balances these two costs.

Evolution:

  Dixon's method (1981):          L[1/2, 2√2]
    Random integers mod N, test for B-smoothness. No structure exploited
    in the choice of integers to test.

  Quadratic Sieve (QS, 1981):     L[1/2, 1]
    Sieve values of (x + ⌊√N⌋)² - N, which are O(√N) in size rather
    than O(N). The smaller values are more likely smooth. Sieving (rather
    than trial division) further accelerates smoothness detection.

  Multiple Polynomial QS (MPQS):  L[1/2, 1] with better constants
    Uses many quadratic polynomials to keep sieved values small.

  Number Field Sieve (NFS, 1993): L[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3} ≈ 1.923]
    The critical innovation: work in a number field Q(α) where α is a
    root of a polynomial f(x) with f(m) ≡ 0 (mod N). Pairs (a, b) are
    sought that are simultaneously smooth over the rational factor base
    AND smooth over the algebraic factor base (norms in the number field).

    Why this helps: norms of a - bα in Q(α) can be much smaller than the
    raw integers in QS, because the polynomial f has degree d > 1, and
    the norm is roughly (a/b)^d · |f(b/a)|, which for well-chosen f and
    sieving region is substantially smaller.

    The three-way balance (see Section 3) between polynomial degree d,
    smoothness bound B, and sieving region size is what produces L[1/3].

  Special Number Field Sieve:     L[1/3, (32/9)^{1/3} ≈ 1.526]
    When N has a special algebraic form (e.g., near a perfect power), a
    polynomial of higher degree with very small coefficients can be chosen,
    reducing norms and shifting the constant from (64/9)^{1/3} down.

  Multiple Number Field Sieve:    L[1/3, ((8/9)(32/9))^{1/3} ≈ 1.902] (heuristic)
    Use several number fields simultaneously to share sieving work.
    The constant drops slightly but remains above (32/9)^{1/3}.

Key point: Every method in this family ultimately needs integers to be
smooth (factorable over a bounded set of primes), and the probability of
B-smoothness is governed by the Dickman–de Bruijn function ρ(u) where
u = (ln M)/(ln B) for M-sized integers tested against bound B. This
function is the fundamental constraint.

Algorithms investigated in this family that do not improve the picture:
- Lattice sieve variants (improve constants, not exponent)
- Large prime variations (1LP, 2LP, 3LP — improve practical performance)
- Coppersmith's block Wiedemann (improves LA, doesn't change α)
- Self-initializing QS variants
- Rational sieve (strictly worse than NFS)
- Various polynomial selection strategies for NFS


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2B. GROUP-ORDER METHODS: L[1/2] IN THE FACTOR SIZE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Core idea: Work in a group whose order depends on an unknown factor p of N.
If you can compute the group order (or a large smooth factor of it), you
can extract p.

  Pollard p-1 (1974):              Polynomial in p if p-1 is smooth
    Works in (Z/NZ)*. Group order mod p is p-1. If p-1 is B-smooth,
    compute a = 2^{B!} mod N, then gcd(a-1, N) reveals p.
    Fails for most cryptographic primes (chosen so p-1 has large factors).

  Williams p+1 (1982):             Polynomial in p if p+1 is smooth
    Works in a quadratic extension. Group order involves p+1.
    Same limitation: requires p+1 to be smooth.

  Elliptic Curve Method (ECM, 1987): L_p[1/2, √2]
    Key insight: instead of a fixed group order (p-1 or p+1), use a
    random elliptic curve E over Z/NZ. By Hasse's theorem, #E(F_p) ∈
    [p+1-2√p, p+1+2√p], and as E varies, #E(F_p) is roughly uniformly
    distributed in this interval. So we get many "chances" — if any
    random curve has a smooth order, we win.

    The probability that a random integer near p is B-smooth gives the
    L_p[1/2] complexity. This is subexponential in p (the factor), not
    in N — making ECM the best method for finding small factors.

  Algebraic tori / twisted curves:  Same complexity class
    Using the group of points on higher-genus curves, algebraic tori
    (T_n quotients), or other algebraic groups gives group orders that
    are polynomials in p (cyclotomic polynomials, etc.), but the
    smoothness probability is still governed by the same Dickman function.
    No algebraic group over F_p has been found whose order avoids the
    smoothness barrier.

Why this family cannot beat NFS for balanced semiprimes: for N = pq with
p ≈ q ≈ √N, the complexity L_p[1/2] ≈ L_N[1/2] with a worse constant
than NFS's L_N[1/3]. ECM is only superior when p is much smaller than N.

Algorithms investigated:
- Higher-dimensional ECM (genus-2 Jacobians, etc.)
- GLS/GLV endomorphism speedups (constant factor only)
- Twisted Edwards / Montgomery parameterizations
- Hyperelliptic curve method
- Lenstra–Lenstra improvement to ECM stage 2


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2C. DIRECT ALGEBRAIC / COMBINATORIAL METHODS: O(N^{1/4}) or worse
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Pollard rho (1975):              O(N^{1/4}) = O(p^{1/2})
    Birthday-paradox collision in (Z/NZ)*. Expected O(√p) steps to find
    a collision mod p, detected via gcd. Extremely low memory (Floyd or
    Brent cycle detection).

  Fermat's method:                 O(N^{1/2}) in worst case
    Write N = x² - y² = (x-y)(x+y). Iterate x from ⌈√N⌉ upward. Only
    efficient when factors are very close together.

  Shanks SQUFOF:                   O(N^{1/4})
    Uses the infrastructure of the principal cycle in a real quadratic
    order. Elegant but same complexity class as Pollard rho.

  Lehman's method:                 O(N^{1/3})

These methods are strictly worse than the smoothness-based family for
large N. They involve no smoothness and no linear algebra, but they also
have no parameter to optimize — there is no analogue of the smoothness
bound that enables the L[α] interpolation.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
3. WHY L[1/3] IS THE BARRIER
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The L[1/3] complexity of NFS is not an accident but the result of a
precise optimization that appears to be tight for all smoothness-based
approaches over the integers.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3A. THE α = 1/(k+1) FORMULA
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Consider a generic sieve-based algorithm with k independent parameters
to optimize (e.g., smoothness bound, sieving region dimensions, polynomial
degree). The resulting complexity, after optimization, takes the form
L[1/(k+1)].

  k = 1 (one parameter: smoothness bound B):
    Dixon / QS: optimal balance gives L[1/2].

  k = 2 (two parameters: B and polynomial degree d):
    NFS: the three-way balance of degree d, smoothness bound B, and
    sieving region gives L[1/3].

    Specifically, with degree d polynomial, norms in the number field
    are roughly N^{1/d}, the smoothness bound is B, and the sieving
    region has volume V. The three constraints:
      (i)   ρ(ln(N^{1/d})/ln B) — smoothness probability
      (ii)  V — number of candidates to sieve
      (iii) π(B)² — size of the factor base (linear algebra cost)
    must balance, and the optimal d grows as (ln N / ln ln N)^{1/3},
    giving the L[1/3] result.

  k = 3 would give L[1/4], but:
    No one has found a third independent parameter to optimize. The
    polynomial degree d is the only "free" structural parameter NFS
    introduced beyond QS's smoothness bound. Every proposed additional
    parameter (multiple number fields, tower extensions, higher-degree
    maps) has turned out to be either dependent on existing parameters
    or unable to reduce norms further.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3B. ARCHIMEDEAN VS NON-ARCHIMEDEAN: WHY FFS BEATS NFS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A crucial comparison: the Function Field Sieve (FFS), which factors
polynomials over finite fields F_q[x], achieves L_q[1/3] and in some
parameter regimes approaches L[0] — nearly polynomial time.

Why can FFS do better? The key difference is the notion of "size":

  Over Z (NFS):    Size is the archimedean absolute value |·|.
                   Norms in number fields are bounded below by the
                   archimedean metric. You cannot make elements "small"
                   in a number field without them actually being small
                   integers (or having small integer coefficients).

  Over F_q[x] (FFS): Size is the degree. The "size" of a polynomial
                   is its degree, a non-archimedean notion. Crucially,
                   you can perform descent — replacing a high-degree
                   element with a product of lower-degree elements —
                   because the "size" function (degree) is compatible
                   with the algebraic operations in a way that the
                   archimedean absolute value is not.

This is why Joux's quasi-polynomial algorithms for discrete log in small-
characteristic finite fields (which use towers of function field extensions
with iterated Frobenius descent) do not transfer to integer factoring.
The Frobenius endomorphism has no analogue over Z. The integers have no
non-trivial endomorphism that reduces archimedean size.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3C. THE THREE-WAY OPTIMIZATION AND THE MNFS FLOOR
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The NFS constant (64/9)^{1/3} comes from the specific balance point. The
Special NFS constant (32/9)^{1/3} ≈ 1.526 comes from the additional
freedom when the polynomial has small coefficients (available only for
numbers of special form).

The Multiple Number Field Sieve (MNFS) uses many number fields to amortize
the sieving cost. In the limit, this approaches but cannot beat the SNFS
constant, because:

  - Using V number fields, each candidate (a,b) pair can be tested for
    smoothness in multiple fields, effectively multiplying the success
    probability by V.
  - But V additional fields require V additional factor bases, inflating
    the linear algebra.
  - The optimal balance, even as V → ∞, converges to a constant strictly
    above (32/9)^{1/3}.

This gives a "floor" for all known NFS variants at L[1/3, (32/9)^{1/3}]
for general integers. No technique within the smoothness paradigm has
breached this floor.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
4. WHAT DOESN'T WORK AND WHY: THE STRUCTURAL OBSTRUCTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Our investigation of ~75 approaches revealed recurring structural reasons
why proposed improvements fail. These obstructions are not mere technical
difficulties but appear to be fundamental features of the integers.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4A. CRT OPACITY: ALL ALGEBRAIC APPROACHES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Chinese Remainder Theorem tells us Z/NZ ≅ Z/pZ × Z/qZ, but this
isomorphism is exactly what we're trying to compute. Any algebraic
structure we build over Z/NZ (polynomial rings, number fields, elliptic
curves, lattices) inherits this opacity:

  - A lattice in Z^n reduced mod N does not "know" about p and q
    individually. LLL/BKZ reduction finds short vectors in the lattice
    mod N, but "short mod N" does not decompose into "short mod p" and
    "short mod q."

  - Gröbner basis computations over Z/NZ are equivalent to factoring:
    the ideal structure of Z/NZ[x₁,...,xₙ] cannot be decomposed without
    knowing the factorization.

  - Resultants, discriminants, and other algebraic invariants computed
    mod N entangle information about both factors in a way that cannot
    be disentangled without splitting N.

This is why "clever algebraic" approaches — expressing factoring as
solving a system of polynomial equations, then applying Gröbner bases /
resultants / elimination theory — do not help. The algebra over Z/NZ is
opaque to the CRT decomposition by construction.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4B. INFORMATION-THEORETIC CONSTRAINTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Several fundamental information-theoretic bounds constrain any factoring
algorithm:

  1 bit per relation:
    Each smooth relation provides exactly 1 bit of information (the
    parity of the exponent vector) toward constructing the GF(2) kernel
    vector. You need π(B) + 1 relations to guarantee a dependency.
    No known way to extract more than 1 bit per smooth relation exists.

  2/√N bits per modular arithmetic probe:
    A single modular multiplication a·b mod N, or a single gcd(a, N)
    computation, reveals at most O(1/√N) bits about the factorization
    (in the information-theoretic sense). This is because the factorization
    is determined by log₂(p) ≈ (1/2)log₂(N) bits, and each arithmetic
    operation constrains the possibilities by at most a constant factor
    relative to √N.

  Birthday bound:
    Any algorithm that collects random elements of a group of order p
    and looks for collisions needs Ω(√p) samples (birthday paradox).
    Pollard rho is optimal in this model.

These bounds are not specific to any algorithm — they apply to the
computational model.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4C. NO Z-ENDOMORPHISM: NO DESCENT OVER THE INTEGERS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The most important structural difference between Z and F_q[x]:

  F_q[x] has the Frobenius endomorphism x ↦ x^q.
    This is a non-trivial ring endomorphism that reduces degrees in
    extension fields. It enables "descent" — expressing a high-degree
    element as a combination of lower-degree elements — which is the
    engine behind the quasi-polynomial discrete log algorithms.

  Z has no non-trivial ring endomorphism.
    The only ring endomorphism of Z is the identity. There is no map
    φ: Z → Z that is a ring homomorphism and reduces archimedean size.

    Consequence: there is no descent procedure over Z. You cannot take a
    large integer and systematically "descend" it to a product of smaller
    integers, because there is no endomorphism to drive the descent. The
    only way to decompose a large integer into smaller factors is to
    actually factor it — which is circular.

This is arguably the single deepest reason why integer factoring appears
harder than discrete logarithms in small-characteristic fields. Every
attempt to import the descent framework from function fields to integers
founders on this absence.

Related attempts that fail:
  - p-adic methods: the p-adic absolute value is non-archimedean, but
    p-adic "size" of an integer n is determined by v_p(n), which gives
    information about divisibility by p — exactly what we're trying to
    learn. Circular.
  - Adelic methods: working in the adele ring simultaneously at all
    places gives no additional structure for factoring, because the
    archimedean place dominates the complexity.
  - Tropical geometry: the tropical semiring (min, +) corresponds to
    valuations, and tropical factoring of a polynomial is easy — but
    lifting tropical factors to actual factors requires solving the
    original problem.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4D. THE EVALUABILITY-CONVERGENCE-SENSITIVITY IMPOSSIBILITY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Several approaches attempt to use analytic or continuous methods to factor:
define a function f(s, N) that encodes the factorization and extract it
by evaluating f. The three requirements for such an approach are:

  Evaluability:   f(s, N) can be computed efficiently for any s.
  Convergence:    f concentrates around the factors (e.g., has poles/zeros
                  at p, q or at related points).
  Sensitivity:    The factors can be read off from the computed values.

The impossibility: no known function satisfies all three simultaneously
for general N.

  - Riemann zeta / L-functions: ζ(s) encodes all primes, but evaluating
    it in the critical strip (where the useful information lives) requires
    Ω(N^{1/6}) operations for N-sized inputs. The analytic continuation
    converges, but not fast enough.

  - Fourier/character sums: Characters χ mod N can detect factors (since
    characters mod N decompose as χ_p ⊗ χ_q), but evaluating the
    relevant character sums requires exhaustive summation.

  - Optimization landscape: Define E(x) = min(N mod x, x - (N mod x))
    and minimize. This has a global minimum at x = p (or q), but the
    landscape is a sawtooth with Θ(N) local minima of depth O(1),
    making gradient methods useless and requiring Ω(√N) random probes
    to find the global minimum.

  - Statistical/spectral methods: The distribution of N mod m for
    various m contains information about factors, but extracting it
    requires m to range over a set of size Ω(N^{1/2-ε}).


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
5. WHAT WOULD BE NEEDED TO BREAK L[1/3]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Based on the structural obstructions identified above, any classical
algorithm achieving o(L_N[1/3]) would need to circumvent at least one
of the fundamental barriers. Here is what that would require:


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5A. A NON-ARCHIMEDEAN "SIZE" NOTION OVER Z
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

If one could define a notion of "size" for integers that:
  (i)   is efficiently computable,
  (ii)  admits a descent procedure (large → product of smaller),
  (iii) is compatible with the ring structure of Z,
then the descent techniques from function fields might transfer.

No such notion is known. The archimedean absolute value is the only
"natural" size on Z, and it admits no descent. The p-adic valuations
are non-archimedean but require knowing p. The height functions used in
arithmetic geometry reduce to the archimedean absolute value for integers.

This is perhaps the most promising (or least hopeless) direction, as it
attacks the deepest structural difference between Z and F_q[x].


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5B. NON-SMOOTHNESS-BASED RELATIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Every known subexponential factoring algorithm ultimately requires finding
smooth numbers — integers all of whose prime factors are below a bound.
The probability of smoothness is governed by the Dickman–de Bruijn function
and cannot be improved.

An alternative would be a relation type that:
  (i)   can be found more efficiently than smooth numbers,
  (ii)  carries enough information to determine the factorization,
  (iii) can be combined (analogous to GF(2) linear algebra) to extract
        factors.

Candidates investigated:
  - "Almost smooth" numbers (smooth except for one or two large primes):
    Already used in NFS (large prime variations). They improve practical
    performance but not the asymptotic complexity class, because the
    large primes still need to be matched, and the matching probability
    is governed by the same smoothness asymptotics.

  - Algebraic relations (e.g., norm equations in number fields): These
    are what NFS already uses. The norms must still be smooth.

  - Lattice-based relations: Finding short vectors in lattices is well
    studied (LLL, BKZ), but the connection to factoring goes through
    smoothness (lattice sieving in NFS finds smooth norms).

No genuinely new relation type has been proposed that avoids smoothness.


───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5C. MORE THAN 1 BIT PER RELATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

If each relation could yield k bits of information (for some k > 1 that
grows with N), then the total number of relations needed would drop by a
factor of k, potentially changing the complexity.

This would require a fundamentally different algebraic structure for
combining relations — something beyond GF(2) linear algebra on exponent
vectors. Working over larger finite fields or using non-linear combination
methods has been explored, but:
  - Over GF(ℓ) for ℓ > 2: you'd need ℓ-th power residuosity, which
    requires ℓ-th roots of unity mod N, available only for ℓ | (p-1)(q-1).
    For random primes, the usable ℓ values are not large enough to help.
  - Non-linear methods: no known way to combine relations non-linearly
    while preserving the ability to extract factors.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
6. HONEST ASSESSMENT: BREAKING L[1/3] CLASSICALLY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Based on the totality of our investigation, we assess the probability of
a classical algorithm achieving o(L_N[1/3]) for general integers.


What we know for certain:
  - L[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3}] is achievable (general NFS).
  - L[1/3, (32/9)^{1/3}] is achievable for special-form numbers (SNFS).
  - No classical algorithm is known below L[1/3] for general N.
  - The L[1/3] barrier has stood for 30+ years (since NFS, ~1993).
  - Shor's algorithm (1994) achieves polynomial time quantumly,
    showing the problem is not "inherently hard" — but the quantum
    approach exploits the group structure of (Z/NZ)* via QFT, which
    has no known classical simulation.

Structural reasons the barrier appears robust:
  1. The α = 1/(k+1) framework requires a new independent optimization
     parameter, and none has been found in three decades.
  2. The absence of Z-endomorphisms blocks the most promising analogy
     (function field descent).
  3. The archimedean nature of integer size appears fundamental and
     un-circumventable within known mathematics.
  4. Multiple independent obstructions (CRT opacity, information-
     theoretic bounds, no descent, evaluability-convergence-sensitivity)
     all point to the same conclusion.
  5. The factoring problem is essentially alone: improvements in related
     problems (discrete log in small-characteristic fields) have not
     transferred to factoring, for well-understood structural reasons.

Reasons for residual uncertainty:
  1. We cannot prove L[1/3] is optimal. No super-polynomial lower bound
     for factoring exists in any reasonable computational model (beyond
     straight-line programs or oracle models).
  2. Major algorithmic breakthroughs have historically been surprises
     (Shor's algorithm, AKS primality test, Joux's quasi-polynomial
     DLP algorithm).
  3. The connection between factoring and lattice problems, algebraic
     geometry, and additive combinatorics is not fully understood.
     A breakthrough could come from unexpected mathematical territory.
  4. Machine-assisted mathematical discovery could find structure in
     the problem that human mathematicians have missed.

Assessment:

  Probability of a classical o(L[1/3]) algorithm for general factoring
  being discovered within the next 20 years:

      ~2-5%

  This estimate reflects:
  - High confidence in the structural barriers (each individually strong,
    and they reinforce each other).
  - Residual humility about unknown unknowns and the history of surprises
    in computational number theory.
  - The observation that the problem has attracted sustained attention
    from the strongest researchers in the field for decades, suggesting
    (but not proving) that the low-hanging fruit has been picked.

  Probability that the NFS constant can be improved below (64/9)^{1/3}
  for general N (while remaining at L[1/3]):

      ~30-40%

  MNFS and future polynomial selection improvements may achieve this.
  The floor appears to be around (32/9)^{1/3} ≈ 1.526.

  Probability that practical factoring of RSA-2048 becomes feasible
  classically within 20 years (through algorithmic + hardware advances):

      ~1-3%

  Even optimistic projections of NFS improvements combined with hardware
  scaling fall far short of the ~2^112 operations estimated for RSA-2048
  with current NFS.

CONCLUSION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The factoring landscape is characterized by a remarkably clean structure:
three families of algorithms, each optimal within its paradigm, converging
on L[1/3] as the classical frontier. The barrier is not a single wall but
an interlocking set of structural obstructions — the archimedean nature
of integer size, the absence of Z-endomorphisms, CRT opacity, and
information-theoretic constraints — that collectively make the integers
uniquely resistant to the techniques that succeed in analogous settings
(function fields, small-characteristic finite fields).

Breaking through would require not an incremental improvement but a
fundamentally new mathematical insight — a new way to measure, decompose,
or relate integers that circumvents the smoothness barrier. Such insights
are rare but not impossible. Until one arrives, L[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3}]
remains the state of the art, and RSA's security rests on the apparent
depth of this barrier.
