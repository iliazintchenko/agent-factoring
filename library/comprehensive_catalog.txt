1. TRIAL DIVISION & BRUTE FORCE  [8 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Useful only as a baseline and for stripping small factors. Completely
  impractical beyond ~20 digits. Serves as verification for other methods.

Overall Verdict: OBSOLETE for research-scale semiprimes (30+ digits).

  1.1  Naive trial division (2,3,5,7,11,...)
       - O(sqrt(N)) divisions. Infeasible past ~20 digits.
  1.2  Wheel-factorization trial division (mod 30, mod 210 wheels)
       - ~3x speedup over naive. Still O(sqrt(N)).
  1.3  Trial division with prime sieve precomputation
       - Sieve primes up to limit, then divide. Memory-bound past 10^10.
  1.4  Segmented trial division
       - Cache-friendly variant. Marginal improvement.
  1.5  Parallel trial division (multi-core, GPU)
       - Linear speedup with cores. Still exponential in digit count.
  1.6  Trial division with modular residue filtering
       - Pre-filter candidates using small modular tests. Modest constant factor.
  1.7  Bounded exhaustive search with GCD batching
       - Batch GCD of N against product trees of primes. Efficient for smooth N.
  1.8  Deterministic complete factorization via precomputed tables
       - Lookup tables for small factors. Only relevant for factors < 10^12.

2. SIEVE-BASED METHODS  [12 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Sieves are the backbone of sub-exponential factoring (QS, NFS). The
  sieving step dominates runtime. Innovations here yield the largest
  practical speedups for 60-100+ digit semiprimes.

Overall Verdict: ESSENTIAL infrastructure. Not standalone methods but
  critical components of QS/NFS pipelines.

  2.1  Sieve of Eratosthenes (baseline prime generation)
  2.2  Segmented sieve for large prime generation
  2.3  Linear sieve (Pritchard)
  2.4  Sieve of Atkin
  2.5  Bucket sieve for large factor base relations (QS/NFS support)
  2.6  Lattice sieve (line sieve generalization for NFS)
  2.7  Special-q sieve for NFS polynomial selection
  2.8  Log-weighted sieve (approximate log sums for smoothness detection)
  2.9  Double large prime sieve variation
  2.10 Triple large prime sieve variation
  2.11 Vector sieve (SIMD-optimized sieve passes)
  2.12 GPU-accelerated sieve kernels

3. FERMAT & ALGEBRAIC FACTORIZATION  [14 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Fermat's method works spectacularly when factors are close together
  (|p-q| small). For random semiprimes where p and q are far apart,
  it degrades to worse than trial division. The algebraic identity
  N = a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b) is the conceptual ancestor of QS and NFS.

Overall Verdict: NICHE USE. Fast for near-equal factors, poor otherwise.
  Conceptually foundational for modern methods.

  3.1  Basic Fermat factorization (search for a^2 - N = b^2)
  3.2  Fermat with multiplier (kN = a^2 - b^2 for various k)
  3.3  Lehman's method (Fermat + trial division hybrid, O(N^{1/3}))
  3.4  McKee's speedup of Fermat's method
  3.5  Hart's one-line factoring algorithm
  3.6  SQUFOF (Square Form Factorization, Shanks)
       - Subexponential heuristic. Good up to ~18 digits. O(N^{1/4}).
  3.7  Difference of squares with Jacobi symbol filtering
  3.8  Algebraic factoring via cyclotomic polynomials
  3.9  Aurifeuillean factorizations
  3.10 Factoring via sum/difference of powers identities
  3.11 Factoring special forms (Mersenne, Fermat numbers, repunits)
  3.12 Multi-polynomial Fermat with sieving
  3.13 Kraitchik's improvement (a^2 ≡ b^2 mod N, not a^2 - N = b^2)
       - Direct ancestor of QS. KEY CONCEPTUAL BRIDGE.
  3.14 Rational sieve (simplest congruence-of-squares method)

4. CONTINUED FRACTIONS (CFRAC)  [10 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  CFRAC was the first general-purpose sub-exponential factoring algorithm
  (Morrison & Brillhart, 1975). It can factor ~50 digit numbers but is
  thoroughly outclassed by QS for anything larger. The convergents of
  sqrt(N) naturally produce small quadratic residues.

Overall Verdict: HISTORICALLY IMPORTANT, now superseded. Good for
  understanding the theory behind QS. Practical up to ~50 digits.

  4.1  Basic CFRAC (Morrison-Brillhart)
       - Complexity: L(N)^{sqrt(2)} where L(N) = exp(sqrt(ln N * ln ln N))
  4.2  CFRAC with early abort strategy
  4.3  CFRAC with multiple polynomials (MPQS precursor idea)
  4.4  CFRAC with large prime variation
  4.5  CFRAC with double large prime variation
  4.6  Class group CFRAC variant
  4.7  Nearest-integer continued fraction expansion
  4.8  CFRAC with optimized factor base selection
  4.9  Parallel CFRAC (independent relation collection)
  4.10 CFRAC with structured Gaussian elimination

5. QUADRATIC SIEVE (QS) FAMILY  [15 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  MPQS (Multiple Polynomial QS) is the most practical algorithm for
  factoring numbers in the 60-100 digit range. Self-initializing MPQS
  (SIQS) is the gold standard implementation. The QS family achieves
  complexity L(N)^1, a major improvement over CFRAC's L(N)^{sqrt(2)}.
  For our dataset (30-100 digits), QS variants are the primary tool.

Overall Verdict: PRIMARY WORKHORSE for 60-100 digit semiprimes. SIQS
  is the recommended implementation. Outperformed by GNFS above ~100 digits.

  5.1  Basic Quadratic Sieve (Pomerance, 1981)
       - Sieve over Q(x) = (x + ceil(sqrt(N)))^2 - N for B-smooth values
  5.2  Multiple Polynomial QS (MPQS)
       - Use many polynomials Q_i(x) to keep sieve values small. ~10x faster.
  5.3  Self-Initializing QS (SIQS / Contini)
       - Fast polynomial switching. Best practical QS variant.
       - RECOMMENDED IMPLEMENTATION for 60-100 digit range.
  5.4  Double Large Prime QS (DLPQS)
       - Allow relations with two large primes. Speeds relation collection ~3x.
  5.5  Triple Large Prime QS
       - Experimental. Marginal gains over DLPQS. Complex cycle-finding.
  5.6  QS with optimal factor base sizing (Silverman's formulas)
  5.7  QS with block sieving (cache-optimized)
  5.8  QS with partial relation combining (cycle-finding in graph)
  5.9  QS with structured Gaussian elimination (Block Lanczos)
  5.10 QS with Block Wiedemann linear algebra
  5.11 QS with filtering / singleton removal / clique purging
  5.12 Parallel QS (distributed relation collection)
  5.13 GPU-accelerated QS sieve step
  5.14 QS with character columns (to find square dependencies)
  5.15 QS with Knuth-Schroeppel multiplier optimization

6. NUMBER FIELD SIEVE (NFS) FAMILY  [14 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  GNFS is asymptotically the fastest known classical factoring algorithm:
  L(N)^{(64/9)^{1/3}} ≈ L(N)^{1.923}. It dominates above ~100 digits.
  However, implementation complexity is enormous. SNFS applies to numbers
  of special algebraic form and is much faster when applicable.
  For our dataset (30-100 digits), NFS begins to outperform QS only at
  the upper end (~95-100 digits).

Overall Verdict: ASYMPTOTICALLY SUPERIOR but massive implementation
  overhead. Crossover with QS around 95-110 digits. Essential for 100+ digits.

  6.1  Special Number Field Sieve (SNFS, Lenstra et al.)
       - For numbers with known algebraic form. L(N)^{(32/9)^{1/3}}.
  6.2  General Number Field Sieve (GNFS)
       - No structural assumptions on N. Best general-purpose algorithm.
  6.3  GNFS with Kleinjung polynomial selection
       - State-of-the-art polynomial selection. Critical for efficiency.
  6.4  GNFS with lattice sieve (Pollard)
  6.5  GNFS with line sieve
  6.6  GNFS with special-q lattice sieve
  6.7  NFS with 2-dimensional sieving
  6.8  NFS with 3-dimensional (3LP) sieving
  6.9  NFS with Block Lanczos linear algebra (over GF(2))
  6.10 NFS with Block Wiedemann (distributed linear algebra)
  6.11 NFS with Coppersmith's modifications
  6.12 NFS square root step (Couveignes, Montgomery)
  6.13 Tower NFS (for discrete log, informing factoring improvements)
  6.14 Multiple NFS (use several number field pairs simultaneously)

7. ELLIPTIC CURVE METHOD (ECM) FAMILY  [12 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  ECM (Lenstra, 1987) has complexity dependent on the SMALLEST factor p,
  not N: roughly L(p)^{sqrt(2)}. This makes it ideal for finding factors
  up to ~60 digits regardless of N's size. For balanced semiprimes
  (where both factors are ~N/2 digits), ECM offers no advantage over QS.
  For our dataset, ECM is useful mainly for the smaller semiprimes (30-50 digits)
  where factors are 15-25 digits.

Overall Verdict: EXCELLENT for small-factor detection. Not competitive
  for balanced semiprimes. Essential as a preprocessing step.

  7.1  Basic ECM (stage 1 only)
  7.2  ECM with stage 2 (continuation, baby-step giant-step)
  7.3  ECM with FFT extension (stage 2.5)
  7.4  ECM with Montgomery curves (faster point arithmetic)
  7.5  ECM with Edwards curves (complete addition law, no exceptions)
  7.6  ECM with Suyama parameterization (guaranteed torsion)
  7.7  ECM with Hessian curves
  7.8  ECM with twisted Edwards curves (optimized for specific torsion)
  7.9  Parallel ECM (independent curves on different cores/GPUs)
  7.10 GPU-ECM (GMP-ECM on CUDA, massive parallelism)
  7.11 ECM with BSGS stage 2 (baby-step giant-step pairing)
  7.12 ECM with optimal B1/B2 bounds (Zimmermann's tables)

8. POLLARD METHODS  [11 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Pollard's rho is the simplest practical factoring method for factors up
  to ~25 digits, with O(p^{1/2}) complexity. Brent's improvement gives a
  constant-factor speedup. Pollard's p-1 is devastating when p-1 is smooth.
  Both are essential preprocessing steps before launching QS/NFS.

Overall Verdict: ESSENTIAL preprocessing. Fast to implement, cheap to run,
  and occasionally solve the problem outright.

  8.1  Pollard's rho (original with Floyd cycle detection)
  8.2  Pollard's rho with Brent's improvement
  8.3  Pollard's rho with Montgomery's GCD batching
  8.4  Pollard's rho with different iteration functions (x^2+c variants)
  8.5  Pollard's rho parallelized (distinguished points method)
  8.6  Pollard's rho on GPU
  8.7  Pollard's p-1 (stage 1)
  8.8  Pollard's p-1 (stage 1 + stage 2)
  8.9  Pollard's p-1 with FFT-based stage 2
  8.10 Pollard's p-1 with multi-stage extensions
  8.11 Pollard's rho with cycle detection via hash table

9. p-1 / p+1 & RELATED SMOOTHNESS METHODS  [10 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  These methods exploit smooth-order subgroups. Williams' p+1 complements
  p-1 by working in a quadratic extension. Success depends entirely on the
  smoothness of p±1, which is unpredictable for random primes. When they
  work, they are dramatically faster than general methods.

Overall Verdict: HIGH VARIANCE. Occasionally spectacular, usually fruitless.
  Always worth trying as preprocessing due to low cost.

  9.1  Williams' p+1 method (Lucas sequences)
  9.2  Williams' p+1 with stage 2
  9.3  Bach-Shallit generalization (p^2-1, p^2+p+1, etc.)
  9.4  p-1/p+1 with ECM stage 2 sharing
  9.5  Higher-power smoothness exploitation (p^k - 1 factorizations)
  9.6  Aurifeuillean factor-guided p±1 attacks
  9.7  Smooth-order group detection via multiple bases
  9.8  Combined p-1, p+1, ECM pipeline (GMP-ECM default strategy)
  9.9  p-1 with Pollard-Strassen algorithm (deterministic, O(N^{1/4}))
  9.10 Smoothness probability analysis for optimal bound selection

10. LATTICE & ALGEBRAIC NUMBER THEORY  [13 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  LLL-based lattice reduction and Coppersmith's method are powerful when
  partial information about a factor is known (e.g., high bits of p).
  Coppersmith can factor N given ~half the bits of p in polynomial time.
  Without side information, lattice methods do not outperform QS/NFS.
  They are critical components within NFS (lattice sieve, polynomial selection).

Overall Verdict: POWERFUL WITH SIDE INFORMATION. Otherwise, supporting
  technology for NFS rather than standalone factoring methods.

  10.1  LLL lattice reduction for approximate factoring
  10.2  Coppersmith's method (small roots of modular polynomials)
        - KEY RESULT: Can factor N given ≥ N^{1/4} bits of p.
  10.3  Boneh-Durfee attack (small private exponent RSA)
  10.4  Herrmann-May extension of Coppersmith
  10.5  Howgrave-Graham's reformulation (integer polynomial roots)
  10.6  Lattice-based partial key exposure attacks
  10.7  Algebraic number field norm-based factoring
  10.8  Ideal factorization in number fields
  10.9  Class group computation-based factoring
  10.10 Lattice sieve for NFS (Pollard's lattice sieve)
  10.11 Joux-Lercier polynomial selection (lattice-optimized)
  10.12 BKZ lattice reduction (stronger than LLL, slower)
  10.13 Multivariate Coppersmith (multiple unknowns)

11. ANALYTIC NUMBER THEORY  [9 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Analytic approaches (Riemann zeta, L-functions, prime distribution)
  provide theoretical insight but have NOT yielded practical factoring
  algorithms. The connection between zeros of zeta and prime distribution
  is deep but does not translate to efficient computation. Smooth number
  probability estimates (Dickman's function) are essential for parameter
  tuning in QS/NFS.

Overall Verdict: THEORETICALLY RICH, PRACTICALLY BARREN for direct
  factoring. Essential for complexity analysis and parameter optimization.

  11.1  Riemann zeta function zero analysis for factor detection
  11.2  Explicit formula approach (prime counting via zeros)
  11.3  Smoothness probability estimation (Canfield-Erdos-Pomerance)
  11.4  Dickman's rho function for optimal QS/NFS parameters
  11.5  Distribution of semiprimes and factor-size statistics
  11.6  Sieve of Eratosthenes analytic extensions (Selberg sieve)
  11.7  Large sieve inequality applications
  11.8  Bombieri-Vinogradov theorem implications for factor distribution
  11.9  Multiplicative function analysis on semiprimes

12. MODULAR ARITHMETIC & GROUP THEORY  [11 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  The structure of (Z/NZ)* and its subgroups is intimately connected to
  factoring. Order-finding (the basis of Shor's algorithm) directly yields
  factors. Classical group-theoretic approaches reduce to known methods
  (p-1, ECM exploit group structure). No novel classical group-theoretic
  method has surpassed NFS.

Overall Verdict: FOUNDATIONAL THEORY. All modern methods exploit group
  structure implicitly. No standalone breakthrough from pure group theory.

  12.1  Random-order method (find ord(a) mod N, hope gcd(a^{ord/2}-1, N) works)
  12.2  Carmichael lambda function estimation
  12.3  Discrete logarithm reduction to factoring
  12.4  Index calculus method (adapted for factoring)
  12.5  Chinese Remainder Theorem reconstruction attacks
  12.6  Hensel lifting for p-adic factoring
  12.7  Quadratic residuosity-based approaches
  12.8  Jacobi/Kronecker symbol filtering
  12.9  Primitive root hunting for structural information
  12.10 Subgroup confinement attacks (small subgroup detection)
  12.11 Pohlig-Hellman decomposition for smooth-order groups

13. PROBABILISTIC & RANDOMIZED METHODS  [8 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Randomization is already core to practical factoring (Pollard's rho,
  random curve selection in ECM, random polynomial selection in QS).
  Pure Monte Carlo approaches without algebraic structure do not compete.
  Birthday-paradox-based methods (rho) are optimal in their class.

Overall Verdict: ESSENTIAL AS INGREDIENTS, not as standalone paradigms.

  13.1  Birthday-paradox factoring (generalized rho)

  13.3  Random walk on factor base elements

  13.5  Monte Carlo factoring with probabilistic smoothness tests
  13.6  Random polynomial evaluation for relation finding
  13.7  Stochastic factor base optimization

14. MACHINE LEARNING & NEURAL APPROACHES  [12 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  ML approaches have shown NO ability to factor numbers beyond what trivial
  methods handle (~15 digits). Neural networks cannot learn the structure
  of factoring in a way that generalizes. The fundamental problem is that
  factoring has no "smooth" landscape for gradient descent — small changes
  in input produce uncorrelated changes in factors. Transformers can
  memorize small-number patterns but do not extrapolate.

Overall Verdict: NEGATIVE RESULTS ACROSS THE BOARD. No ML approach has
  demonstrated any advantage over classical methods for any digit count
  in our dataset (30-100 digits). This is likely a fundamental limitation.

  14.1  Feed-forward neural network (binary input → binary factor output)
  14.2  Recurrent neural network (digit-by-digit factor prediction)
  14.3  Transformer model for factorization (sequence-to-sequence)
  14.4  Reinforcement learning for factoring strategy selection
  14.5  Graph neural network on factor-relation graphs
  14.6  Neural network-guided ECM curve selection
  14.7  ML-optimized QS parameter tuning (factor base size, sieve interval)
  14.8  Deep learning for smoothness prediction
  14.9  Genetic programming for factoring function discovery
  14.10 Diffusion model for factor distribution estimation
  14.11 Variational autoencoder for semiprime structure
  14.12 Hybrid ML + classical pipeline (ML for preprocessing only)

15. QUANTUM & QUANTUM-INSPIRED  [10 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Shor's algorithm factors in polynomial time on a fault-tolerant quantum
  computer. Current quantum hardware (noisy, <1000 logical qubits) cannot
  factor numbers beyond ~20 digits. Quantum-inspired classical algorithms
  (tensor networks, simulated annealing) do not achieve quantum speedups.
  For our dataset, no quantum approach is practically viable in 2026.

Overall Verdict: THEORETICALLY DEVASTATING, PRACTICALLY UNAVAILABLE.
  Shor's algorithm is the only known polynomial-time factoring method.
  Not usable on current or near-term quantum hardware for 30+ digit numbers.

  15.1  Shor's algorithm (order-finding via quantum Fourier transform)
  15.2  Shor's algorithm with Beauregard's circuit optimization
  15.3  Quantum approximate optimization (QAOA) for factoring
  15.4  Variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) for factoring
  15.5  Adiabatic quantum computation for factoring
  15.6  Quantum annealing (D-Wave) for factoring as optimization
  15.7  Simulated quantum annealing (classical simulation)
  15.8  Tensor network contraction for quantum circuit simulation
  15.9  Regev's recent algorithm (multidimensional quantum Fourier transform)
       - Reduces qubit count but increases circuit depth. Theoretical.
  15.10 Quantum-inspired tensor decomposition methods

16. OPTIMIZATION & METAHEURISTIC METHODS  [14 approaches]
================================================================================

Most Important Finding:
  Formulating factoring as an optimization problem (minimize |N - p*q|)
  and applying metaheuristics (genetic algorithms, simulated annealing,
  ant colony, etc.) consistently fails for numbers beyond ~20 digits.
  The search landscape is astronomically large with no useful gradient.
  These methods explore O(population_size * generations) candidates, which
  is negligible compared to the factor space for 30+ digit numbers.

Overall Verdict: UNIFORMLY UNSUCCESSFUL. The optimization landscape for
  factoring has no exploitable structure for metaheuristics. These methods
  cannot compete with even basic trial division for numbers in our dataset.

  16.1  Genetic algorithm (chromosome = candidate factor)
  16.2  Simulated annealing for factoring
  16.3  Particle swarm optimization
  16.4  Ant colony optimization
  16.5  Differential evolution

================================================================================
                        KEY CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

1. PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATIONS FOR THIS DATASET (30-100 digit semiprimes):

   - 30-50 digits: ECM (GMP-ECM) with standard bounds, or Pollard's rho
     for the smaller end. Most can be factored in seconds to minutes.

   - 50-80 digits: SIQS (Self-Initializing Quadratic Sieve) is the method
     of choice. Implementations: YAFU, msieve, or custom SIQS.
     Expected runtimes: seconds (50d) to hours (80d).

   - 80-100 digits: SIQS remains competitive but GNFS begins to win at
     the upper end. The crossover point is implementation-dependent,
     typically around 95-110 digits.
     Expected runtimes: hours (80d) to days/weeks (100d) for QS;
     GNFS may be faster above ~95 digits with good polynomial selection.

2. WHAT DOES NOT WORK (for 30-100 digit balanced semiprimes):

   - Machine learning: No generalization capability for factoring.
   - Metaheuristics: Search space too large, no useful gradient.
   - Quantum: Hardware decades away from factoring 30+ digit numbers.
   - Exotic methods: No demonstrated advantage over classical algorithms.
   - Analytic number theory: Insights help tune parameters but don't
     directly factor numbers.

3. OPEN QUESTIONS:

   - Is there a classical algorithm with complexity better than L(N)^{1.923}?
   - Can Regev's quantum approach lead to practical implementations
     before full fault-tolerant quantum computing?
   - Can ML meaningfully assist parameter selection for QS/NFS, even if
     it cannot factor directly?
   - Are there structural properties of specific semiprimes in our dataset
     (e.g., smooth p±1) that make them vulnerable to special methods?

4. RECOMMENDED PIPELINE FOR ATTACKING THIS DATASET:

   Step 1: Trial division to 10^6                        [< 1 second]
   Step 2: Pollard's rho (Brent variant, 10^6 iterations) [seconds]
   Step 3: Pollard's p-1 (B1=10^6, B2=10^9)              [seconds]
   Step 4: ECM (B1=optimal for target factor size)        [seconds-hours]
   Step 5: SIQS (for 50-95 digit N)                       [minutes-days]
   Step 6: GNFS (for 95-100 digit N, if SIQS too slow)    [hours-weeks]

- "Investigated" means the approach was analyzed for theoretical
  applicability, implemented (where feasible), and tested against the
  semiprimes in our dataset or representative numbers of similar size.

- For impractical approaches (quantum, exotic), investigation consisted
  of theoretical analysis and literature review rather than implementation.

- Runtime estimates assume commodity hardware (modern multi-core CPU,
  consumer GPU where applicable).

- Complexity notation: L(N) = exp(sqrt(ln N * ln ln N)). Sub-exponential
  methods have complexity L(N)^c for constant c.
